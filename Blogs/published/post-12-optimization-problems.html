The Setup Freeze

There is a specific moment in every optimization AP Calculus problem where students who know derivatives cold suddenly go quiet. It happens before the derivative. Before the algebra. It happens in the first 90 seconds, when the exam hands you a word problem and there is no equation written out yet. You are staring at a scenario about a box, or a fence, or a can, and your brain is asking one question on repeat: "What do I even write first?"

I call it the Setup Freeze. And it is not a calculus problem. It is a translation problem.

Every student I have ever worked with can take a derivative. That is not the issue. The AP FRQ optimization section is where scores separate because the exam hands you a context, not an equation. You have to build the equation yourself before any derivative happens. Most AP prep resources skip directly to the derivative step. That gap is exactly what this post closes.

This post covers AP Calculus optimization problems from the first word of a setup through the final answer with correct justification. If you have been scoring fine on derivative rules but losing points on applied FRQs, this is why. And if the AP exam is coming up soon, Unit 5 accounts for 15 to 18 percent of your score. That is not a section you can afford to treat as a bonus.

What Optimization Actually Means

Most students hear "optimization" and think: take a derivative and set it equal to zero. That part is right. But it is the last third of the process, not the whole thing.

Here is the analogy I use with every student I tutor. Imagine you are packing a car for a road trip. You have a fixed trunk space and you want to fit as many bags as possible without going over. You cannot just start shoving bags in. You need to know the trunk dimensions first (constraint), figure out what you are maximizing (objective), and then figure out what you can actually control (variable). The derivative only comes in when you are ready to find the best possible arrangement.

That is optimization. There is always something to maximize or minimize calculus is tracking (the objective), always something fixed that limits your options (the constraint), and always a variable you control.

In AP Calculus, those three pieces show up in every derivative application problem. The formal definition: optimization is the process of finding the absolute maximum or absolute minimum value of a function on a given domain.

The word "absolute" matters here. You are not looking for a local high point somewhere in the middle. You want the actual best value across the entire domain. A local max is just higher than its immediate neighbors. An absolute max is the highest point on the whole domain. On an AP FRQ, the exam asks you to justify which one you found. Your justification is what earns the point, not the number itself.

The 8-Step Optimization Framework

This is the process I walk every student through before they touch a single derivative. Write it down. The calculus optimization steps below map onto every problem type you will see on the AP exam.

  • Identify what to maximize or minimize.

    Read the problem and underline the quantity that needs to be as large or as small as possible. Volume, area, cost, distance, profit. Write it at the top of your work in words before writing any variable.

  • Name your variables.

    Pick letters and define them. "Let $x$ be the width in centimeters. Let $h$ be the height in centimeters." If you skip this, your work cannot earn justification points even when your math is right.

  • Write the primary equation (the objective function).

    Express what you are optimizing in terms of your variables. You need a formula before you can do anything with it.

  • Write the constraint equation. Do not skip this.

    Every optimization problem has a constraint: total fencing is 240 meters, surface area is 300 square centimeters, total cost must stay under a budget. Write it as an equation. No constraint means no way to reduce to one variable.

  • Reduce to one variable using the constraint.

    Solve the constraint for one variable and substitute into the objective function. After this step, your objective function has only one variable.

  • Find critical points on the AP exam by setting the derivative equal to zero.

    Take the derivative and set it equal to zero. Solve for your variable. This is where the instantaneous rate of change equals zero. The AROC (average rate of change) of your objective function across an interval gives a rough picture of behavior. The derivative (the instantaneous rate of change) pinpoints exactly where the maximum or minimum occurs. Finding where $f'(x) = 0$ means asking: where does the rate of change hit exactly zero? That is a Unit 2 concept living inside a Unit 5 problem.

  • Classify using the First or Second Derivative Test.

    Finding a critical point is not enough. Confirm whether it is a maximum, minimum, or neither. The second derivative test optimization rule: if $f''(c) < 0$, you have a local max. If $f''(c) > 0$, you have a local min.

  • Check endpoints if the domain is a closed interval.

    If the problem specifies a closed interval, use the closed interval method calculus requires: evaluate the objective function at both endpoints AND at your critical point. The largest value is the absolute max. The smallest is the absolute min. Do not skip endpoints.

Average Rate of Change (AROC)
$\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}$
Tells you how fast the objective changes over an interval. Not where the max/min is.
Interval: not what locates extrema
Instantaneous Rate of Change (IROC)
$f'(x) = 0$
This is what the derivative gives you at a single point. Setting it to zero is how you find the max/min.

Worked Example 1: The Box Problem (Foundational)

This is the most common optimization problem type in AP Calculus AB. Expect a version of it on the FRQ or in related derivative application problems.

Problem Setup

A rectangular piece of cardboard is 12 cm by 12 cm. Equal squares of side length $x$ are cut from each corner. The sides are folded up to form an open box. Find the value of $x$ that maximizes the volume of the box.

Step 1: Objective

Maximize volume $V$.

Step 2: Variables

Let $x$ = side length of each cut corner, in cm. Domain: $0 < x < 6$ (corners cannot exceed half the sheet dimension).

Step 3: Objective Function

After cutting and folding: height = $x$, length = $12 - 2x$, width = $12 - 2x$.

$$V(x) = x(12 - 2x)^2$$

Step 4: Constraint

The domain constraint $0 < x < 6$ is already embedded. No separate constraint equation is needed here since the problem was originally set up in one variable.

Steps 5–6: Reduce and Differentiate

Expand $V(x)$:

$$V(x) = x(144 - 48x + 4x^2) = 144x - 48x^2 + 4x^3$$
$$V'(x) = 144 - 96x + 12x^2$$

Set $V'(x) = 0$ and divide by 12:

$$x^2 - 8x + 12 = 0 \implies (x-2)(x-6) = 0$$

Critical points: $x = 2$ and $x = 6$. Since $x = 6$ is the endpoint (gives zero volume), the only interior critical point is $x = 2$.

Step 7: Second Derivative Test

$$V''(x) = -96 + 24x \implies V''(2) = -96 + 48 = -48$$

Since $V''(2) = -48 < 0$, the function is concave down at $x = 2$. This is a local maximum.

Step 8: Check Endpoints

The absolute maximum volume is 128 cm³ when $x = 2$ cm.

Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Correct objective function with all dimensions written out: 1 point
  • Correct derivative with algebra shown: 1 point
  • Correct critical point(s) identified: 1 point
  • Classification justified (second derivative test OR sign chart, not just stated): 1 point
  • Correct answer with units: 1 point
  • Endpoint check included (or domain stated as open with justification): 1 point

The justification step is where students leave points on the table. Writing "$x = 2$ is a maximum" without showing the second derivative test or a sign chart earns zero on the classification point. The AP grader needs to see the reasoning.

Worked Example 2: Full FRQ-Level Problem

This is the kind of AP FRQ optimization problem that shows up as a full free-response question, with multiple parts requiring setup, calculus, and written justification.

FRQ-Level Problem

A farmer has 240 meters of fencing to build a rectangular enclosure. One side of the enclosure is a barn wall, so it does not need fencing. Find the dimensions that maximize the enclosed area.

Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Correct objective function: 1 point
  • Correct constraint equation written and used: 1 point
  • Derivative of single-variable function: 1 point
  • Correct critical point: 1 point
  • Written justification (reasoning, not just computed): 1 point
  • Correct final dimensions with units: 1 point

Setup

Let $x$ = width (two sides of fencing), $y$ = length (one side, parallel to the barn wall).

Objective Function

$$A = xy$$

Constraint Equation

$$2x + y = 240$$

Reduce to One Variable

Solve the constraint for $y$:

$$y = 240 - 2x$$

Substitute into the area function:

$$A(x) = x(240 - 2x) = 240x - 2x^2$$

Domain: $0 < x < 120$ (both $x$ and $y$ must be positive).

Find the Critical Point

$$A'(x) = 240 - 4x = 0 \implies x = 60$$

Justify Using the Second Derivative Test

$$A''(x) = -4 \implies A''(60) = -4 < 0$$

Concave down at $x = 60$: local maximum. Only critical point on $(0, 120)$, so this is the absolute maximum.

First Derivative Sign Chart (Alternative Justification)

Interval$A'(x)$Behavior of $A$
$(0, 60)$positiveincreasing
$(60, 120)$negativedecreasing

Answer

$$x = 60 \text{ m}, \quad y = 240 - 120 = 120 \text{ m}$$
$$A_{\max} = 60 \times 120 = \boxed{7200 \text{ m}^2}$$
FRQ Communication Layer: The AP rubric requires a written justification sentence, not just numbers. Here is what full-credit looks like: "Since $A'(x) = 240 - 4x$ changes from positive to negative at $x = 60$, by the First Derivative Test, $A(x)$ has a local maximum at $x = 60$. Because $x = 60$ is the only critical point in the interval $(0, 120)$, this local maximum is also the absolute maximum." Write that sentence. Do not just circle the answer.

The 3 Mistakes That Cost My Students Points

Mistake 1: Skipping the Constraint Equation
Why it happens: Students see the objective function and go straight to the derivative. The problem states a total, and students treat it as background information instead of a mathematical equation they need to write down.
Without the constraint, you cannot reduce to one variable. You end up with a derivative that has two unknowns and no way to solve it. This alone can cost 2 to 3 points on a 6-point FRQ. I have watched students who set up the objective correctly lose half the problem's points because they never wrote a single constraint equation.
Mistake 2: Not Checking Endpoints on Closed Interval Problems
Why it happens: Students find a critical point, classify it, and stop. The closed interval method says you must also evaluate at both endpoints.
The absolute maximum or minimum might be at an endpoint, not at the critical point. That is a fully wrong answer with mostly correct algebra. On derivative application problems with a closed interval, this mistake appears in roughly one out of three student FRQ attempts I review.
Mistake 3: Justifying with Words Alone, or Math Alone
Why it happens: Students either write "this is a max because the second derivative is negative" without showing the computation, or show $V''(2) = -48$ with no sentence explaining what that means.
The AP rubric expects both. The computation and a sentence connecting it to the conclusion. Writing "since $f''(c) < 0$, $f$ has a local maximum at $c$" is what earns the point. Just showing the number does not. This costs exactly 1 point per problem and adds up across the full exam.
📲

Set up your constraint equation before you touch the derivative, or you are already losing points.

Practice Problems

Try these on your own before checking the answers. If you get stuck, go back to the 8-step framework and identify exactly where your setup breaks down. That is usually the constraint step.

Problem 1: Fencing with Divider

A rancher has 400 feet of fencing to enclose a rectangular area, then divide it in half with a fence parallel to one side. Find the dimensions of the full enclosure that maximize the total area.

Problem 2: Cylinder Cost

A cylindrical can is to hold $250\pi$ cubic centimeters. The top and bottom material costs twice as much per square centimeter as the side material. Find the radius that minimizes total material cost.

Problem 3: AP Exam Style (Closed Interval)

A particle moves along the x-axis with position $x(t) = 2t^3 - 9t^2 + 12t$ for $0 \le t \le 4$.

(a) Find all critical points on $[0, 4]$.

(b) Find the absolute maximum and minimum positions using the closed interval method.

(c) Justify your answer in one complete sentence referencing the Candidates Test.

Answers
Problem 1: Three sides of fencing: $3x + 2y = 400$. Area: $A = xy$. Solving: $x = \frac{200}{3} \approx 66.7$ ft, $y = 100$ ft. Max area $\approx 6{,}667$ ft².
Problem 2: Cost function: $C(r) = 4\pi r^2 + \frac{500\pi}{r}$. Setting $C'(r) = 0$ gives $r = \sqrt[3]{62.5} \approx 3.97$ cm.
Problem 3: (a) Critical points at $t = 1$ and $t = 2$. (b) Values: $x(0) = 0$, $x(1) = 5$, $x(2) = 4$, $x(4) = 32$. Absolute max = 32 at $t = 4$; absolute min = 0 at $t = 0$. (c) "By the Candidates Test, comparing $x(0) = 0$, $x(1) = 5$, $x(2) = 4$, $x(4) = 32$ shows the absolute maximum is 32 at $t = 4$ and the absolute minimum is 0 at $t = 0$."

FAQ

Do I have to use the second derivative test, or can I use a sign chart instead?

Either works for full credit on the AP exam. The second derivative test optimization is faster when $f''(c)$ is easy to evaluate. The first derivative sign chart is more reliable when the second derivative is complex or when you are on a closed interval and need to compare endpoint values. I personally prefer sign charts for AP Calculus optimization problems because they also show the behavior of the function around the critical point, which makes your written justification easier to write.

What you cannot do is state a conclusion without showing either test. The AP grader needs to see the evidence.

What is the difference between a local max and an absolute max? Does the AP exam test both?

Yes, and it tests them separately. A local maximum is a point where the function is larger than all nearby points. An absolute maximum is the largest value the function takes on the entire domain. An absolute max might not be at a critical point at all. It might be at an endpoint.

The closed interval method calculus requires compares all candidates: every critical point plus both endpoints. If the AP exam asks for the absolute maximum and you only check critical points, you may get the wrong answer. I see this mistake in a majority of my students' first practice FRQ attempts.

What if the second derivative test is inconclusive?

Go to the first derivative sign chart. If $f''(c) = 0$, the test says nothing useful. Check whether $f'$ changes sign at $c$. Positive to negative means local max. Negative to positive means local min. No sign change means an inflection point, not an extremum.

This shows up occasionally on the AP exam, and students who only know the second derivative test get stuck. Learn both. The sign chart never fails you.

Can optimization appear in the MCQ section, or is it always FRQ?

Both. The MCQ version typically gives you a pre-built function and asks which $x$ value maximizes or minimizes it, or asks you to identify the sign of the derivative at a specific point. The FRQ version requires the full setup from a word problem. For MCQ, derivative application problems are manageable once you recognize the form. For FRQ, the setup is what decides your score.

This post focuses on the FRQ because that is where points are actually lost. The MCQ version is mostly a critical points identification question in disguise.

  • $f''(c) < 0$ at critical point $c$: local maximum
