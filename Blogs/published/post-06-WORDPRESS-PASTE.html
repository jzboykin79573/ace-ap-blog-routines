The Mistake I See Every Single Year

Roughly half my students get the related rates FRQ wrong every spring not because they cannot take a derivative, but because they substitute their numbers before they differentiate, and that single move quietly costs them 3 to 4 points every May.

I watch this happen year after year. A student sits down with a cone-filling problem. They know the formula is $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$. They plug in the specific numbers they were given. Then they try to differentiate. By then the damage is done. The derivative becomes impossible to take.

Here is the thing: every student I have seen do this is smart enough to pass AP Calculus. They understand derivatives. They know how to use the chain rule. The problem is not intelligence. The problem is order. Do the steps in the wrong order and a solvable problem becomes unsolvable. Do them in the right order and it takes 90 seconds.

Related rates is not a new concept. It is implicit differentiation with real numbers attached. And yet it gets a whole section of the exam because the order of operations matters. More than any other problem type in Unit 4, related rates separates students who actually practiced this specific skill from students who thought they did.

This post teaches you exactly where to put the numbers and when. By the time we are done, you will know the five-step process cold, you will see why that order is critical, and you will be ready for any rate problem the AP exam throws at you. Including the ones with changing angles, changing dimensions, and multiple variables all moving at once.

That is the difference between a 3 and a 5 on the free response.

What Related Rates Actually Means

In your Unit 2 post, we talked about the distinction between average rate of change and instantaneous rate of change. Related rates is where that distinction becomes essential.

Average rate of change tells you how much something changed over an interval. A ladder falling 2 feet over 3 seconds. A cone filling 5 cubic centimeters per second. Those are just numbers. They do not connect to anything.

Related rates asks: if one thing is changing at a known rate, how fast is something else changing at this exact moment?

Your car accelerates from 0 to 60 miles per hour. At the exact moment you hit 60, what is your acceleration? That is a related rate question. The speed changed, and we want to know the rate of change of speed right now.

Same thing with the cone. Water flows into a cone at 2 cubic centimeters per second. That is constant. The volume is always growing at that rate. But the height is not growing at a constant rate. At the moment the water level is 3 centimeters high, the height is growing at some rate. At 5 centimeters, it is growing at a different rate. Related rates finds that instantaneous rate.

That distinction matters. Most students think "the cone is filling at 2 cubic centimeters per second, so the height is growing at... something." The something is the whole problem. And the only tool that finds it is implicit differentiation.

Why This Matters: A Real Scenario from the Exam

Picture yourself on FRQ question 2. You have 40 minutes left. The problem says: "Water flows into a cone-shaped container at a constant rate of 2 cubic centimeters per second. The cone has a fixed height of 10 centimeters and a radius of 4 centimeters. Find the rate at which the water level is rising when the water depth reaches 3 centimeters."

Your instinct is correct: you should use a formula and take a derivative. But here is where students diverge.

Half the room thinks: "The problem gives me a rate (2 cm³/s) and specific numbers (3 cm, 10 cm, 4 cm). Let me plug those in right now." They write $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$, then substitute $h = 3$, getting $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 (3)$. Then they try to differentiate. But $h$ is no longer a variable. The derivative of 3 is zero. Their equation breaks.

The other half thinks: "I need to find a relationship between the variables first, then differentiate, then substitute." They identify that the water forms a cone with the same proportions as the full cone (similar triangles). They write $r = \frac{2}{5}h$. They substitute into the volume formula before any numbers touch the problem, getting $V = \frac{4\pi}{75}h^3$. Now $h$ is still a variable. They differentiate. Then they plug in the numbers. They solve correctly in 90 seconds.

The difference is order. And the difference in score is 3 to 4 points.

Wrong Order — Loses 3 Points
1. Write $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$
2. Substitute $h = 3$ immediately
3. Try to differentiate
— $h$ is gone. You need $\frac{dr}{dt}$ and you don't have it. Stuck.
Right Order — Full Credit
1. Write $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$
2. Eliminate $r$ using similar triangles
3. Differentiate, then substitute $h = 3$
— $h$ is still a variable when you differentiate. Solvable.
Numbers are dessert. Eat the geometry and calculus first — substitute only at the very end.

The Working Definition

Related Rates — Working Definition

Related rates is a method that uses implicit differentiation with respect to time to find an unknown rate of change when other rates are known.

Implicit differentiation — differentiate the constraint equation as-is, do not solve for one variable first. With respect to time — every changing variable gets a $\frac{d\cdot}{dt}$ term. Unknown rate — that is what you are solving for. Other rates are known — that is what the problem gives you.

The Method: Five-Step Framework

Every related rates problem follows the same order. Get the order wrong and you get the wrong answer. Get it right and you get a 9 out of 9 points on the FRQ.

1
Draw a diagram and label all variables.

Not a fancy diagram. A sketch. A circle for the cone. A triangle for the ladder. A rectangle for the trough. Anything that helps you see the relationships. Label every dimension with a variable. Height is $h$. Radius is $r$. Time is always $t$.

Do not label any dimension with a number yet. That is the step students skip that costs them points.

2
Write down what you know and what you need to find.

The problem will tell you a rate. Water flows in at $\frac{dV}{dt} = 2$ cm³/s. The ladder is sliding down at $\frac{dx}{dt} = -1$ foot/second. Write these down.

At the bottom of your paper, write what you are solving for. Maybe $\frac{dh}{dt}$ at a specific moment. Maybe $\frac{dr}{dt}$. Be clear about what you need.

3
Write an equation relating all variables.

This is the geometry step. Use the formulas you should have memorized: volume of a cone, area of a circle, Pythagorean theorem, similar triangles.

Write one equation that connects everything moving in the problem. For a cone filling: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$. For a ladder sliding: $x^2 + y^2 = L^2$.

4
Differentiate both sides with respect to time using implicit differentiation.

Differentiate the whole equation. Use chain rule for anything involving a variable that changes over time.

Do not substitute numbers yet. Differentiate first, substitute second. This order is the difference between a solvable problem and an unsolvable one.

5
Substitute known values and solve for the unknown rate.

Now plug in the specific numbers from the problem. Height at that moment. Known rates. Everything. Then solve for the rate you need.

The Order That Never Changes
  • Geometry first — draw, label all variables, eliminate extras using constraints
  • Differentiate second — implicit differentiation with respect to time, chain rule on every term
  • Numbers last — substitute specific values only after the derivative is complete

Worked Example 1: The Cone Problem (Foundational)

This is the most common related rates problem on the AP exam. Water flows into a cone. The radius and height change together. Your job: find the rate at which the height is growing. This problem type accounts for about 60% of related rates FRQs historically, so mastering this specific setup is worth the effort.

Problem

Water flows into a cone-shaped container at a constant rate of 2 cubic centimeters per second. The cone has a fixed height of 10 centimeters and a fixed radius of 4 centimeters (measured at the top opening). At the moment when the water level reaches 3 centimeters high, how fast is the water level rising? Express your answer in exact form and as a decimal approximation.

Understanding the Setup Before You Solve

Before you write anything, pause and picture what is happening. Water is flowing in at the same rate the entire time: 2 cm³/s, constant. But the cone is narrow at the bottom and wider at the top. When the water is 1 centimeter high, the water surface is a tiny circle. When the water is 5 centimeters high, the water surface is a larger circle. Same volume per second flowing in, but a wider surface, so the height grows more slowly. This is the insight that makes related rates real: one constant rate ($\frac{dV}{dt}$) produces a changing rate ($\frac{dh}{dt}$). That is the whole problem.

Fig. 1 — Cone with apex at bottom. The full cone has fixed height 10 cm and radius 4 cm. The water inside has changing height h and surface radius r. Similar triangles give the ratio r / h = 2 / 5 at every moment.

1
Draw and label — keep all dimensions as variables

Sketch the cone with apex at the bottom (see diagram above). Inside it, draw the smaller cone representing the current water level. Label the water height $h$ and the water surface radius $r$. The full cone dimensions (10 cm, 4 cm) are fixed constants. Do not write specific numbers on your diagram yet.

2
Write down what you know and what you need to find

Known: $\frac{dV}{dt} = 2$ cm³/s (constant rate water flows in)

At the moment of interest: $h = 3$ cm (the water depth right now)

Find: $\frac{dh}{dt}$ when $h = 3$ (how fast is the height growing right now?)

3
Write the volume equation and eliminate the extra variable

The volume of a cone is $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$. But this has two changing variables — $r$ and $h$ — which means you cannot differentiate cleanly yet. Use similar triangles to eliminate $r$.

The water cone and the full cone are similar shapes. The full cone has $\frac{r}{h} = \frac{4}{10} = \frac{2}{5}$, so at any moment:

$$r = \frac{2}{5}h$$

Substitute into the volume formula:

$$V = \frac{1}{3}\pi \left(\frac{2}{5}h\right)^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi \cdot \frac{4}{25}h^2 \cdot h = \frac{4\pi}{75}h^3$$

Now the equation has only $h$ as a changing variable.

4
Differentiate both sides with respect to time

Differentiate $V = \frac{4\pi}{75}h^3$ with respect to $t$. Apply chain rule on $h^3$ since $h$ changes over time:

$$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4\pi}{75} \cdot 3h^2 \cdot \frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{4\pi h^2}{25} \cdot \frac{dh}{dt}$$

No numbers yet. $h$ is still a variable. $\frac{dh}{dt}$ is the unknown you are solving for.

5
Substitute known values and solve

Plug in $\frac{dV}{dt} = 2$ and $h = 3$:

$$2 = \frac{4\pi (3)^2}{25} \cdot \frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{36\pi}{25} \cdot \frac{dh}{dt}$$
$$\frac{dh}{dt} = 2 \cdot \frac{25}{36\pi} = \frac{50}{36\pi} = \frac{25}{18\pi} \text{ cm/s}$$
$$\boxed{\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{25}{18\pi} \approx 0.44 \text{ cm/s}}$$
Zach's Note — Units cost you points. The AP exam awards a specific scoring point for correct units on every rate answer. Write cm/s (centimeters per second), not just the number. If the problem asks for a volume rate, write cm³/s. If it asks for an area rate, write cm²/s. One missing unit label can cost you a full point on an FRQ worth 9. Write your units every single time.

Why the height grows slowly at h = 3: The water is only 3 cm deep out of 10, so the water surface is a narrow circle. The cone is getting wider as the water rises. By the time the water reaches 5 cm, the same 2 cm³/s is spreading over a larger surface, so $\frac{dh}{dt}$ will be smaller. One constant volume rate, one changing height rate. That is the whole insight.

How the AP exam scores this problem
  • Point 1: Geometric relationship — You must state or clearly show $\frac{r}{h} = \frac{4}{10}$ (similar triangles). The relationship must appear explicitly.
  • Point 2: Volume equation in one variable — You substitute the relationship and simplify to $V = \frac{4\pi}{75}h^3$ or equivalent.
  • Point 3: Correct implicit differentiation — You differentiate with respect to time and apply chain rule: $\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4\pi h^2}{25} \cdot \frac{dh}{dt}$ or equivalent.
  • Point 4: Substitute and solve — Plug in $\frac{dV}{dt} = 2$ and $h = 3$, then solve algebraically. Arithmetic must be correct.
  • Point 5: Units and correct notation — Write $\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{25}{18\pi}$ cm/s. Not $h' = 0.44$. The AP is strict on notation and units.

Worked Example 2: The Ladder Problem (FRQ-Level)

This is the second most common related rates problem on the AP exam, accounting for about 25% of related rates questions. A ladder leans against a wall. The bottom slides away at a constant rate. The top slides down at a different rate. Two things moving simultaneously. Your job: find one rate when you know the other. This is harder than the cone because there is no similar-triangles step — the constraint comes directly from geometry, and you differentiate immediately.

Why This Problem Is Harder Than the Cone

In the cone problem, you had to eliminate a variable using similar triangles before differentiating. Here, you do not. The Pythagorean theorem directly relates $x$ and $y$. You can differentiate it immediately. But that means $\frac{dy}{dt}$ appears alongside $\frac{dx}{dt}$ in your derivative equation. You must be comfortable with multiple rates in one equation.

Problem

A 13-foot ladder leans against a vertical wall. The bottom of the ladder slides away from the wall at a constant rate of 2 feet per second. At the moment when the bottom of the ladder is 5 feet from the wall, how fast is the top of the ladder sliding down the wall?

Fig. 2 — Ladder leaning against a wall. The horizontal distance x and vertical distance y both change with time. The ladder length L = 13 ft is constant, so it has zero derivative.

1
Draw, label, and identify what is fixed vs. what changes

Sketch a right triangle (see diagram above): wall is vertical, ground is horizontal, ladder is the hypotenuse.

  • $x$ = horizontal distance from the wall to the base of the ladder (changes over time)
  • $y$ = vertical distance from the ground to the top of the ladder (also changes)
  • The ladder length is 13 feet — this never changes

The ladder length is a constant. Its derivative will be zero. That is why the constraint equation stays simple.

2
Write down what you know and what you need to find

Given: $\frac{dx}{dt} = 2$ feet/second (the bottom slides away at a constant rate)

At the specific moment: $x = 5$ feet (the bottom is currently 5 feet from the wall)

Find: $\frac{dy}{dt}$ when $x = 5$ (how fast is the top sliding down right now?)

3
Write the constraint equation and find the missing dimension

By the Pythagorean theorem, at any moment in time:

$$x^2 + y^2 = 13^2 = 169$$

Before you differentiate, find the value of $y$ when $x = 5$ — you need it for Step 5:

$$25 + y^2 = 169 \implies y^2 = 144 \implies y = 12 \text{ ft}$$
4
Differentiate both sides with respect to time

Apply the chain rule to both terms on the left. The right side is zero because 169 is a constant (the ladder length never changes):

$$2x \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + 2y \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} = 0$$
5
Substitute known values and solve for the unknown rate

Plug in $x = 5$, $y = 12$, $\frac{dx}{dt} = 2$:

$$2(5)(2) + 2(12) \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} = 0$$
$$20 + 24 \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{20}{24} = -\frac{5}{6} \text{ ft/s}$$
$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{5}{6} \text{ ft/s} \approx -0.833 \text{ ft/s}}$$
Zach's Note — Units and sign both matter. The negative sign is not a mistake — it tells you the height is decreasing, which is exactly what should happen as the ladder slides away. Write ft/s (feet per second) with your answer, not just the number. On the AP, leaving off the unit label or the sign costs you a point. Both are meaningful. The answer is $-\frac{5}{6}$ ft/s, which means the top of the ladder is sliding DOWN at $\frac{5}{6}$ ft/s.

Interpreting the Answer: What the Negative Sign Means

The negative sign tells you that $y$ is decreasing. As the ladder slides away from the wall, the top slides down. A decreasing quantity has a negative rate of change. On the AP exam, you can express this answer in two equivalent ways:

  • Formal notation: $\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{5}{6}$ feet per second
  • Contextual language: The top of the ladder is sliding down at a rate of $\frac{5}{6}$ feet per second.

The AP accepts both. Most students use the formal notation in their work and the contextual form in the final answer sentence when the rubric asks for one.

How the AP exam scores this problem
  • Point 1: Constraint equation — You must write $x^2 + y^2 = 169$ explicitly. Skipping this and going straight to differentiation loses this point.
  • Point 2: Find the missing dimension — Substitute $x = 5$ into the Pythagorean equation and correctly solve for $y = 12$. Many students skip this step, which makes Step 5 impossible.
  • Point 3: Correct implicit differentiation — Differentiate both sides, apply chain rule to both $x^2$ and $y^2$: $2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0$. Forgetting chain rule on $y^2$ loses this point.
  • Point 4: Substitute and solve — Plug in $x = 5$, $y = 12$, $\frac{dx}{dt} = 2$, solve for $\frac{dy}{dt}$. Arithmetic must be correct including fraction simplification.
  • Point 5: Sign, units, and notation — Write $\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{5}{6}$ ft/s. The negative sign and the units are both required for this point.

Why Related Rates Appears on the FRQ Every Year

When the AP exam writers design the free response section, they are not trying to trick you. They are trying to test whether you understand calculus as a tool for the real world. Related rates is perfect for this because it requires every skill from Unit 4: geometric constraint reasoning, implicit differentiation, the chain rule, and algebraic problem-solving, all in sequence.

What the AP Is Actually Measuring

When you solve a related rates FRQ, the grader is assessing five things:

  1. Can you translate a word problem into a mathematical constraint? Identifying what is fixed vs. what is changing is not calculus — it is mathematical modeling. Many students skip this step or do it carelessly.
  2. Do you know the geometric formulas and when to use them? The AP assumes you know these cold. No formula sheet. No guessing.
  3. Can you eliminate extra variables before differentiating? In the cone problem, you must use similar triangles. This is not asked for explicitly — you must recognize that you need it. This is the highest-level reasoning step.
  4. Do you know implicit differentiation well enough to apply it flawlessly? Chain rule on every changing term, every time.
  5. Can you execute algebra under pressure without losing track of variables? Fractions, simplification, sign errors — the arithmetic matters.
Related rates is the only problem type on the AP that measures modeling, formula knowledge, constraint reasoning, differentiation, and algebra — all in one question. That is why it appears every year.

Historical Pattern: Related Rates on the AP

  • 100% of recent exams include a related rates problem or implicit differentiation problem that uses the same technique.
  • About 70% of those are explicit word problems (cones, ladders, shadows).
  • About 30% are dressed up as pure calculus ("implicitly differentiate and find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ at a specific point").

Cone and ladder are the archetypes. Every other related rates problem on the AP is a blend of these two. Master those two setups and you can solve anything the exam throws at you.

The 3 Mistakes That Cost My Students Points

Over 15 years of teaching AP Calculus, I have seen the same errors tank students on this FRQ repeatedly. These three mistakes are not conceptual. They are execution errors. And they are completely predictable.

Mistake 1: Substituting Numbers Before Differentiating
Why it happens: The problem gives you specific numbers and your instinct is to plug them in immediately. It feels efficient.

You see the cone formula $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$, you read that the water level is 3 centimeters, and you think: "I should substitute $h = 3$ right now." So you write $V = \pi r^2$ and try to differentiate. But 3 is now a constant multiplier. You end up needing $\frac{dr}{dt}$, which the problem did not give you. You are stuck. 2 to 3 points gone in 30 seconds.

The correct order:

  1. Write the general equation: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$
  2. Eliminate extra variables using geometry: $r = \frac{2}{5}h$
  3. Substitute the constraint into the volume formula: $V = \frac{4\pi}{75}h^3$
  4. Differentiate: $\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4\pi h^2}{25} \cdot \frac{dh}{dt}$
  5. Now substitute $h = 3$ and solve.

Memory rule: Geometry first. Differentiation second. Numbers last. Think of the numbers as dessert. You do not eat dessert before the meal.

This is the number one error on related rates FRQs. It costs 2 to 3 points on a 9-point question — the exact gap between a 4 and a 5 on the composite score.

Mistake 2: Forgetting the Chain Rule on Variables That Change Over Time
Why it happens: You see $r^2$ and apply power rule: $\frac{d}{dt}(r^2) = 2r$. But $r$ is a function of time, not a constant.

The correct derivative is $\frac{d}{dt}(r^2) = 2r \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}$. The chain rule adds the $\frac{dr}{dt}$ at the end. Without it, your equation is missing a term and your final answer is wrong.

Fix: Before you differentiate, draw a box around every variable that changes with time. In the cone problem, box $r$ and $h$. The ladder length? Do not box it — it is fixed. Every boxed variable gets a $\frac{d(\text{variable})}{dt}$ when you differentiate it.

Missing this on one term costs 1 to 2 rubric points. Missing it on multiple terms means your entire differentiation is wrong and your answer cannot be recovered.

Mistake 3: Identifying the Wrong Geometric Constraint
Why it happens: Students memorize "cone = similar triangles" and "ladder = Pythagorean" without understanding why.

Then they see a shadow problem they have never seen before and guess the wrong constraint. If the constraint is wrong, the whole problem is wrong — 2 to 3 points gone immediately.

Fig. 3 — Shadow problem. The street light and the person create two similar triangles. The constraint comes from matching ratios of corresponding sides.

Fix: Do not memorize constraints for specific problems. Memorize the method instead.

  1. Draw a picture. A sketch that shows the geometric setup.
  2. Identify what is fixed. Write down the fixed quantities.
  3. Identify what is changing. Write down which quantities change.
  4. Write the geometric relationship. Right triangle with changing sides? Pythagorean theorem. Two similar shapes with a fixed ratio? Similar triangles. Circle or sphere changing? Area or volume formula.
  5. Write one equation that relates all the variables and constants.

The shadow problem above uses similar triangles — exactly like the cone. A student who drew a picture and saw two triangles would identify this. A student who memorized "cone = similar triangles" might guess wrong. Draw the picture every time. Always.

Practice Problems

Try these on your own before checking the answers. If you get stuck, go back to the method section and identify which step you are stuck on.

Problem 1 — Cone with water

Water flows into a cone at 5 cubic inches per second. The cone has a fixed height of 12 inches and a fixed radius of 6 inches. At the moment when the water level is 4 inches high, how fast is the water level rising?

Problem 2 — Circle expanding

A circle's area is increasing at a constant rate of 10 square centimeters per second. At the moment when the radius is 3 centimeters, how fast is the radius increasing?

Problem 3 — Two ships

Two ships leave a harbor. One travels due north at 20 miles per hour. The other travels due east at 15 miles per hour. At the moment when the northbound ship has traveled 12 miles and the eastbound ship has traveled 9 miles, how fast is the distance between them increasing?

Answers
Problem 1: Similar triangles give $r = \frac{1}{2}h$, so $V = \frac{\pi}{12}h^3$. Differentiating: $\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{\pi h^2}{4} \cdot \frac{dh}{dt}$. Substituting $\frac{dV}{dt} = 5$ and $h = 4$: $\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{5 \cdot 4}{4\pi \cdot 4} = \frac{5}{4\pi} \approx 0.398$ in/s.
Problem 2: $A = \pi r^2$, so $\frac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi r \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}$. Substituting $\frac{dA}{dt} = 10$ and $r = 3$: $\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{10}{2\pi(3)} = \frac{5}{3\pi} \approx 0.53$ cm/s.
Problem 3: First find distance: $d = \sqrt{12^2 + 9^2} = \sqrt{225} = 15$ miles. Then $d^2 = x^2 + y^2$, differentiate: $2d\frac{dd}{dt} = 2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt}$. Substituting: $2(15)\frac{dd}{dt} = 2(12)(20) + 2(9)(15) = 480 + 270 = 750$, so $\frac{dd}{dt} = \frac{750}{30} = 25$ mi/hr.

FAQ

Q: Why do I have to use implicit differentiation? Can I just solve for one variable first?

Short answer: you can try, but it usually makes things worse. In the cone, solving for $r$ first worked because similar triangles gave a clean relationship. In the ladder problem, isolating $y$ from $x^2 + y^2 = 169$ gives $y = \sqrt{169 - x^2}$ — which is more complicated to differentiate and more error-prone than the implicit approach.

On harder problems with multiple variables, solving first is impossible. Implicit differentiation works on all problems without modification. Use it every time.

Q: What does the negative sign mean in the ladder problem?

A negative rate means the quantity is decreasing. When $\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{5}{6}$, the height is decreasing at $\frac{5}{6}$ feet per second. The sign gives direction. On the AP exam you must interpret the sign correctly — answering "$\frac{5}{6}$ feet per second" without the negative loses the units/sign point on the rubric.

  • Notation form: $\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{5}{6}$ feet per second
  • Contextual form: The height is decreasing at $\frac{5}{6}$ feet per second.
Q: What if I get a positive rate for something that should be decreasing?

Check your setup. You probably mislabeled a direction. The physics of the problem should match your answer. If the ladder top is sliding down, $\frac{dy}{dt}$ must be negative. Common mistake: writing $\frac{dx}{dt} = -2$ when the problem says "the bottom slides away at 2 feet per second." Sliding away is the positive direction, so $\frac{dx}{dt} = +2$.

Q: Do I need to memorize all the geometric formulas?

Yes, and I am serious about this. The AP exam does not provide a formula sheet. Write these on the top of your exam as soon as you sit down:

  • Cone: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$
  • Cylinder: $V = \pi r^2 h$
  • Sphere: $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$, $A = 4\pi r^2$
  • Circle: $A = \pi r^2$, $C = 2\pi r$
  • Pythagorean theorem: $x^2 + y^2 = c^2$

This takes 90 seconds and saves you mental energy when you are mid-problem and panicking about whether the cone formula has a $\frac{1}{3}$.

Q: What if the problem has more than two variables changing?

Use the exact same method. Write one constraint equation relating all variables. Differentiate with respect to time. The differentiated equation will have multiple $\frac{d\cdot}{dt}$ terms. Plug in the known rates and solve for the unknown. The two-ship problem in the practice section is an example: three changing quantities ($x$, $y$, $d$), one constraint ($d^2 = x^2 + y^2$), same five-step method.

Q: Can I use implicit differentiation without understanding the geometry?

Technically yes, but you will be fragile. You could follow the steps mechanically and get the right answer on a familiar problem. But on the exam, when you hit a problem you have not seen before, geometric intuition is what lets you debug. Draw the picture. Understand why the cone height grows fast when the water is shallow and slow when it is deep. Understand why the ladder height decreases as the base moves away. That intuition separates students who can solve any related rates problem from students who can only solve the one they practiced.

  • Write units on every rate answer (cm/s, ft/s, cm³/s, etc.)
  • Include the sign in your answer — negative means decreasing
Never Do This
  • Substitute a specific number into the equation before you differentiate
  • Forget the $\frac{d\cdot}{dt}$ on variables inside functions (chain rule)
  • Differentiate a constant and expect a non-zero answer
  • Leave off units on the final rate answer
