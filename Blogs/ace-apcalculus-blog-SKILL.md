## 2. ZACH'S VOICE — NON-NEGOTIABLE RULES

### POV Structure
- **I** = Zach teaching, sharing experience, giving opinions
- **We** = Zach and the student working through something together ("let's look at this")
- **You** = addressing the student directly at all times
- Never third person. Never "students should..." Always "I" or "you."

### Tone
- Knowledgeable older sibling who actually cares
- Direct, warm, confident — never preachy
- Short sentences. Real talk. No fluff.
- Never textbook. Never professor voice.

### Forbidden words and phrases
dive into, let's explore, it's worth noting, non-negotiable, unpack, in conclusion, straightforward, navigate, crucial, "from a graphical standpoint," "it is worth noting," "in math terms" (as an opener)

### Forbidden punctuation
Em-dashes (—). Use a period or rewrite. Zero exceptions.

### Never start a section with a definition
Always open with: a student scenario, a mistake Zach sees, a contradiction, or Zach's opinion.

## 3. THE 5-LAYER HUMANIZATION SYSTEM

Every paragraph must be audited against these 5 layers before finalizing. Score 0-10. Flag anything under 7.

### Layer 1 — Opinion Injection
Zach must have a take. Not just facts. What does Zach believe, prefer, or recommend — and why?
- Bad: "The derivative is the slope of the tangent line."
- Good: "Textbooks explain this with secant lines. I have never found that helpful. Here is what I use instead."

### Layer 2 — Contradiction Move
Flip the expected. Say the thing the reader does not expect.
- Bad: "Definition 1 is the most important..."
- Good: "You would think Definition 1 is the most important since it shows up first. It is not."

### Layer 3 — Real Numbers and Specifics
Ground every claim in a concrete number, name, or example. Vague = AI.
- Bad: "Most students only learn one definition."
- Good: "Most students only learn 1, if not any! The AP exam uses all 3."
- Bad: "It costs them points."
- Good: "I have had students lose 4 to 6 points on a single free response because of this."

### Layer 4 — Ugly Truth
An honest admission that most resources skip. The cost of not doing this. The gap most students have.
- Bad: "Make sure you know all three definitions."
- Good: "If you cannot say that definition out loud without looking at your notes, you are not ready for the exam."

### Layer 5 — Voice and Readability
Short sentences. Natural rhythm. No textbook phrasing. Would Zach say this in a tutoring session?
- Bad: "From a graphical standpoint, that question has a clean answer."
- Good: "On a graph, that question has a clean answer."

### Fast Audit Format (use for ALL paragraphs — full rewrite loop only for flagged paragraphs under 7)
```
Para [X] — [section name]
Scores: O:[x] C:[x] N:[x] U:[x] V:[x]
Flags: [layer] — [one line fix]
Priority: [one sentence]
AI Risk: Low/Medium/High
Rewrite: [full paragraph]
```

### AI Risk Indicators — High Risk Phrases to Eliminate
- "From a graphical standpoint"
- "It is worth noting"
- "In math terms" as a paragraph opener
- "This section will cover"
- "By the end of this post"
- "Let's explore"
- Numbered deliverable lists in the intro
- Any sentence that lists what the post will do instead of doing it

## 4. KEY LESSONS FROM THE HUMANIZATION AUDIT (POST 1)

These are the specific patterns discovered by running the 5-layer audit on the first full blog post:

### What AI does that Zach does not
1. Opens sections with definitions instead of scenarios or contradictions
2. Uses vague stakes ("costs them points") instead of specific numbers ("4 to 6 points")
3. Describes what a post will do instead of doing it (high AI risk in intros)
4. Lists deliverables in bullet form where Zach would say it conversationally
5. Uses passive or formal transitions: "from a graphical standpoint," "in math terms"
6. Writes rubric boxes as plain lists with no Zach voice opener
7. Ends paragraphs without consequences — states a fact and stops

### What Zach's voice actually sounds like (extracted from transcripts and audit)
1. Opens with "Every year, I watch..." or "Here is something I tell every one of my students..."
2. Uses real student outcomes: "scoring 80s and 90s on unit tests and still freezing"
3. States his opinion directly: "In my opinion, this is the single most forgotten concept in Unit 2"
4. Uses contrast: "Not similar. The same." / "Not 1. Not 2. All 3."
5. Closes with consequences: "That is the gap that costs you points on the free response every single time"
6. Uses numerals not words for impact: "3 definitions" not "three definitions"
7. Adds exclamation for genuine surprise: "Most students only learn 1, if not any!"
8. Speaks to parents naturally in CTAs without a jarring shift in tone

### Paragraph-level rules learned
- **Intro**: Lead with a real student failure scenario + specific student profile (score range). Add opinion. Add contradiction. Give the post promise with a consequence if they skip it.
- **Analogy sections**: Open with what the textbook gets wrong, then give Zach's version. The analogy earns more when framed as a deliberate choice.
- **Definition sections**: Open with the personal/ugly truth about what happens when students skip this. Use real numbers (points lost). Then give definitions.
- **Each definition card**: Open with a contradiction or opinion, not the definition itself.
- **Worked examples**: Every rubric box opens with "Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem" — not a generic label.
- **Mistakes section**: Each "why it happens" gets a second sentence with Zach's take on why it is actually understandable, then the fix adds a consequence.
- **Practice problems**: Add a coaching opener. "Try these on your own before checking the answers. If you get stuck, go back to the definition section and identify the form first."
- **FAQ**: Every answer closes with a Zach line. Not just the fact. A coaching close.
- **End CTA**: Add one consequence line. "Because if this is shaky, everything after it gets harder than it needs to be."

### MCQ vs. FRQ framing by post type
- For posts covering **limit definitions, definition recognition, or form identification**: frame the payoff as **multiple choice** points. These are 1-point automatic wins for students who know the forms. Do not frame these as FRQ-heavy.
- For posts covering **worked procedures** (power rule, chain rule, integration by parts, etc.): FRQ framing is appropriate since these are extended computation problems.
- Default rule: if the skill is "recognize the form and answer," it is MCQ. If the skill is "set up, execute, and justify," it is FRQ.

### AROC vs. IROC — standing flag
Any post that touches rate of change, velocity, slope, or tangent lines must surface the average vs. instantaneous distinction at least once. This contrast is tested directly across Unit 2, Unit 4, Unit 5, and both FRQ types. It is never wrong to name it explicitly.

## 7. MATHJAX FORMATTING

- Inline math: `$f(x) = x^2$`
- Display math: `$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]$$` on its own line
- Fractions: `\frac{numerator}{denominator}`
- Derivatives: `\frac{dy}{dx}` or `f'(x)`
- Integrals: `\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx`
- Limits: `\lim_{x \to a} f(x)`
- Square roots: `\sqrt{x}`
- Boxed answers: `\boxed{f'(x) = 2x}`

## 8. DESMOS GRAPH RULES

### In the .md draft
Use this spec block anywhere a graph is needed. WordPress gets a screenshot uploaded manually from the HTML preview.

```
[DESMOS EMBED:
Functions: [expression] (color [hex])
Point: [coordinate] labeled "[label]" (color [hex])
Window: x[min,max] y[min,max]
Lock viewport: yes/no
Notes: [any additional context]]
```

### In the HTML preview
The preview generator translates the spec above into a live interactive Desmos embed using the Desmos JavaScript API. No screenshots needed for preview — the graph renders live.

Standard embed template:
```html
<div id="desmos-[id]" style="width:100%;height:320px;border-radius:10px;overflow:hidden;margin:16px 0;"></div>
<script src="https://www.desmos.com/api/v1.7/calculator.js?apiKey=dcb31709b452b1cf9dc26972add0fda6"></script>
<script>
  var elt = document.getElementById('desmos-[id]');
  var calc = Desmos.GraphingCalculator(elt, {
    expressions: false, settingsMenu: false, zoomButtons: false, lockViewport: true
  });
  calc.setMathBounds({ left: [xmin], right: [xmax], bottom: [ymin], top: [ymax] });
  calc.setExpression({ id: 'curve', latex: '[f(x)]', color: '#1a3a6b', lineWidth: 2.5 });
  calc.setExpression({ id: 'tangent', latex: '[g(x)]', color: '#e8a020', lineWidth: 2 });
  calc.setExpression({ id: 'point', latex: '([x],[y])', color: '#e8a020', pointSize: 9 });
  calc.setExpression({ id: 'label', latex: '([x],[y])', label: '[label text]', showLabel: true, color: '#e8a020' });
</script>
```

Design system colors: navy `#1a3a6b`, gold `#e8a020`.
