## 2. ZACH'S VOICE — NON-NEGOTIABLE RULES

### POV Structure
- **I** = Zach teaching, sharing experience, giving opinions
- **We** = Zach and the student working through something together ("let's look at this")
- **You** = addressing the student directly at all times
- Never third person. Never "students should..." Always "I" or "you."

### Tone
- Knowledgeable older sibling who actually cares
- Direct, warm, confident — never preachy
- Short sentences. Real talk. No fluff.
- Never textbook. Never professor voice.

### Forbidden words and phrases
dive into, let's explore, it's worth noting, non-negotiable, unpack, in conclusion, straightforward, navigate, crucial, "from a graphical standpoint," "it is worth noting," "in math terms" (as an opener)

### Forbidden punctuation
Em-dashes (—). Use a period or rewrite. Zero exceptions.

### Never start a section with a definition
Always open with: a student scenario, a mistake Zach sees, a contradiction, or Zach's opinion.

### Voice Technique Rules

**Rule 1 — First sentence speaks to the moment.**
Name exactly what the student is feeling or doing right now. Not a generic situation. "It's April and you're staring at your composite score wondering if a 5 is still possible" is correct. "AP Calculus is a challenging course" is not.

**Rule 2 — Say the uncomfortable truth.**
Write the thing other blogs won't say. "If it's April and you haven't done a single timed FRQ, you're behind. Here's how to make up the ground fast." If you cannot find the uncomfortable truth in a section, the section is not done yet.

**Rule 3 — Dry, specific humor only.**
Not broad absurdism. Exasperated specificity. "The related rates cone-filling problem has been on this exam since before your parents were in high school." One moment per post maximum. It must earn its place by being genuinely specific, not just wry.

**Rule 4 — No hedging language.**
"It might be helpful to consider" becomes "Review this." "You may want to think about" becomes "Think about this." Be decisive. Any sentence that hedges is a sentence that has not been finished yet.

**Rule 5 — Paragraph length: 2-4 sentences maximum.**
This applies at every weight level. A paragraph that runs 5+ sentences is not one thought. It is two thoughts that have not been separated. Cut it.

**Rule 6 — One moment of warmth, earned.**
Do not open with cheerleading. Give the real content first. After the work has been done, one honest human line is allowed. "You already know more than you think." That line lands because it comes after real instruction, not before it.

## 3. THE 5-LAYER HUMANIZATION SYSTEM

The 5 layers define what makes Zach's voice real. They apply at the **section level**, not the paragraph level. Every section must collectively carry the layers its weight profile requires. Not every paragraph needs every layer — that is what makes writing sound human instead of formulaic.

### The 5 Layers (definitions unchanged)

**Layer 1 — Opinion Injection**
Zach must have a take somewhere in the section. Not just facts. What does Zach believe, prefer, or recommend — and why?
- Bad: "The derivative is the slope of the tangent line."
- Good: "Textbooks explain this with secant lines. I have never found that helpful. Here is what I use instead."

**Layer 2 — Contradiction Move**
Flip the expected. Say the thing the reader does not expect. One contradiction per section is enough. Two is too many.
- Bad: "Definition 1 is the most important..."
- Good: "You would think Definition 1 is the most important since it shows up first. It is not."

**Layer 3 — Real Numbers and Specifics**
Ground every claim that makes a stake in a concrete number, name, or example. Vague = AI. Applies even in process paragraphs: use actual values, not placeholder variables.
- Bad: "Most students only learn one definition."
- Good: "Most students only learn 1, if not any! The AP exam uses all 3."

**Layer 4 — Ugly Truth**
An honest admission that most resources skip. The cost of not doing this. Used once per HEAVY section. Not needed in process sections.
- Bad: "Make sure you know all three definitions."
- Good: "If you cannot say that definition out loud without looking at your notes, you are not ready for the exam."

**Layer 5 — Voice and Readability**
Short sentences. Natural rhythm. No textbook phrasing. Applies to every paragraph at every weight level. This is the minimum bar — even a process paragraph must pass Layer 5.
- Bad: "From a graphical standpoint, that question has a clean answer."
- Good: "On a graph, that question has a clean answer."

**Layer 6 — Name the Feeling**
Students have unnamed AP Calculus experiences. Name them with precision. The FRQ Freeze. The false-confidence spiral after easy MCQs. The "I studied this" blank-out during the actual exam. Give the feeling a name, describe it precisely, show why it matters. This is not required at every section. It belongs once per post in a HEAVY section, typically the intro or the Mistakes section. A section that names a feeling earns more credibility than a section that just describes a problem.
- Bad: "Many students struggle with the free response section."
- Good: "There's a specific kind of panic that hits when you read an FRQ and your mind goes blank on something you practiced 20 times. I call it the FRQ Freeze. It happens because timed practice feels different from untimed review, and most students only do untimed."

Layer 6 is not audited mechanically. Zach's subjective pass checks for it once per post.

---

### Paragraph Types

Every paragraph in the post is one of three types. The type determines which layers apply.

**HEAVY paragraph** — carries 2+ layers (always L1 or L4, plus L5). Used in HEAVY sections. Maximum 1-2 per section. These are Zach's real takes. They earn their weight because they follow context that builds to them.

**LIGHT paragraph** — short, direct, first person. Layer 5 only. No stance required. "Those cancel. Boom." is a complete, valid paragraph. The default paragraph type for most of the post.

**PROCESS paragraph** — inside worked examples. Clear math, clear steps. Layer 3 applies (use real numbers in the problem). Layer 5 applies (no textbook phrasing). The Zach voice lives in the intro sentence and coaching close around the example, not inside the algebra itself.

Run the audit per section, not per paragraph. Flag the section if required layers are missing, or if a LIGHT/PROCESS section has HEAVY paragraphs stacked into it.

```

Layers present: O:[present/missing] C:[present/missing] N:[present/missing] U:[present/missing] V:[present/missing] F:[present/missing — Intro only; N/A for all other sections]
Required by profile: [which layers this section's weight requires]
Flag: [any layer missing that this section requires] OR [HEAVY paragraph found in LIGHT section]
Rewrite: [the specific paragraph that needs adjustment, if any]
```

Example of a correct flag:
```
SECTION Worked Example 1 — PROCESS
Layers present: O:present C:present N:present U:missing V:present
Required by profile: L3 + L5 only
Flag: L1 (opinion) and L2 (contradiction) present but NOT required — 2 heavy paragraphs found in a PROCESS section. Strip opinion from the algebra walkthrough. Move any coaching note to the closing sentence only.
```

### AI Risk Indicators — High Risk Phrases to Eliminate

These patterns appear at any weight level and always signal AI:
- "From a graphical standpoint"
- "It is worth noting"
- "In math terms" as a paragraph opener
- "This section will cover"
- "By the end of this post"
- "Let's explore"
- Numbered deliverable lists in the intro
- Any sentence that lists what the post will do instead of doing it
- 3+ consecutive paragraphs all opening with "I" + opinion statement (stacked heavy = AI pattern)

## 4. KEY LESSONS FROM THE HUMANIZATION AUDIT (POST 1)

These are the specific patterns discovered by running the 5-layer audit on the first full blog post:

### What AI does that Zach does not
1. Opens sections with definitions instead of scenarios or contradictions
2. Uses vague stakes ("costs them points") instead of specific numbers ("4 to 6 points")
3. Describes what a post will do instead of doing it (high AI risk in intros)
4. Lists deliverables in bullet form where Zach would say it conversationally
5. Uses passive or formal transitions: "from a graphical standpoint," "in math terms"
6. Writes rubric boxes as plain lists with no Zach voice opener
7. Ends paragraphs without consequences — states a fact and stops

### What Zach's voice actually sounds like (extracted from transcripts and audit)
1. Opens with "Every year, I watch..." or "Here is something I tell every one of my students..."
2. Uses real student outcomes: "scoring 80s and 90s on unit tests and still freezing"
3. States his opinion directly: "In my opinion, this is the single most forgotten concept in Unit 2"
4. Uses contrast: "Not similar. The same." / "Not 1. Not 2. All 3."
5. Closes with consequences: "That is the gap that costs you points on the free response every single time"
6. Uses numerals not words for impact: "3 definitions" not "three definitions"
7. Adds exclamation for genuine surprise: "Most students only learn 1, if not any!"
8. Speaks to parents naturally in CTAs without a jarring shift in tone

### Paragraph-level rules learned
- **Intro**: Lead with a real student failure scenario + specific student profile (score range). Add opinion. Add contradiction. Give the post promise with a consequence if they skip it.
- **Analogy sections**: Open with what the textbook gets wrong, then give Zach's version. The analogy earns more when framed as a deliberate choice.
- **Definition sections**: Open with the personal/ugly truth about what happens when students skip this. Use real numbers (points lost). Then give definitions.
- **Each definition card**: Open with a contradiction or opinion, not the definition itself.
- **Worked examples**: Every rubric box opens with "Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem" — not a generic label.
- **Mistakes section**: Each "why it happens" gets a second sentence with Zach's take on why it is actually understandable, then the fix adds a consequence.
- **Practice problems**: Add a coaching opener. "Try these on your own before checking the answers. If you get stuck, go back to the definition section and identify the form first."
- **FAQ**: Every answer closes with a Zach line. Not just the fact. A coaching close.
- **End CTA**: Add one consequence line. "Because if this is shaky, everything after it gets harder than it needs to be."

### MCQ vs. FRQ framing by post type
- For posts covering **limit definitions, definition recognition, or form identification**: frame the payoff as **multiple choice** points. These are 1-point automatic wins for students who know the forms. Do not frame these as FRQ-heavy.
- For posts covering **worked procedures** (power rule, chain rule, integration by parts, etc.): FRQ framing is appropriate since these are extended computation problems.
- Default rule: if the skill is "recognize the form and answer," it is MCQ. If the skill is "set up, execute, and justify," it is FRQ.

### AROC vs. IROC — standing flag
Any post that touches rate of change, velocity, slope, or tangent lines must surface the average vs. instantaneous distinction at least once. This contrast is tested directly across Unit 2, Unit 4, Unit 5, and both FRQ types. It is never wrong to name it explicitly.

### Variety rules — Post 02 lessons

These rules were added after Post 02 read as formulaic. Every paragraph carried equal weight. That is an AI pattern, not a writing pattern.

- **A heavy paragraph is earned, not scheduled.** You cannot drop an ugly truth in the middle of an algebra walkthrough — there is no setup for it. Heavy paragraphs land because the reader was led to them. Context first. Stance second.
- **3+ heavy-style paragraphs in a row = AI pattern.** If three consecutive paragraphs all open with Zach's opinion or a contradiction, that is stacked stance. Real writing alternates: heavy, then light, then process, then maybe heavy again.
- **Light is not lazy. Light is clean.** "Those cancel. Boom." is a complete paragraph. "Here is what that gives us:" followed by the result is a complete paragraph. Short, direct, first person. No stance required.
- **Process sections are where the student does the work.** Do not interrupt worked examples with philosophy. Coach before the example (one sentence). Coach after the example (one sentence). Inside the algebra: just the steps.
- **The 5 layers belong to the section, not the paragraph.** If the intro section collectively has opinion, contradiction, real numbers, ugly truth, and voice — it passes. It does not matter that the third sentence is pure explanation.
- **FAQ answers are factual first.** The Zach close at the end of each FAQ answer is the one heavy move. The answer itself should be direct and correct. Do not philosophize inside the answer.
- **Variety is the signal.** A post with natural variety makes the intro feel urgent, the examples feel like a working session, and the mistakes section feel like a coaching conversation. Each section should have a different emotional register.

## 5. BLOG POST STRUCTURE

This structure defines the long-form post type. For other post types, see Section 13 for structural modifications. Apply Section 13 overrides on top of this base.

Always follow this exact structure. The weight profile for each section is listed in brackets — follow it exactly. See Section 3 for what each weight level means.

```
[TITLE] — format: [Topic]: [Student Benefit or FRQ Angle]
[READING TIME + SOCIAL PROOF]
[TABLE OF CONTENTS] — "Jump To What You Need"

[INTRO — 100-150 words] ← WEIGHT: HEAVY
  Open with a real student scenario or mistake, NOT a definition.
  Hook format: must be one of the 4 types defined in Section 14. Declare the type in a comment on the line above the first sentence: <!-- HOOK TYPE: [type] -->
  Story-before-principle required: ground any major claim in a real student scenario first, then the lesson, then the exact action. Never principle first.
  Require: L1 + L2 + L3 + L4 + L5 + L6 collectively across the section.
  Foundation consequence required: why struggling here bleeds into later units.

[WHAT THIS TOPIC ACTUALLY MEANS — 150-200 words] ← WEIGHT: LIGHT + 1 STANCE
  Analogy first, formal definition last.
  One contradiction or opinion paragraph. The rest is clear explanation.
  Do not stack opinion across every paragraph.

[THE METHOD / STEP-BY-STEP FRAMEWORK — 200-300 words] ← WEIGHT: LIGHT
  Process delivery. Short, clear steps. No forced opinion.
  Real numbers in every example (L3). Clean voice throughout (L5).
  No philosophy inside the steps.

[WORKED EXAMPLE 1 — FOUNDATIONAL — 300-400 words] ← WEIGHT: PROCESS
  One intro sentence (LIGHT). Algebra walkthrough (PROCESS). One coaching close (LIGHT).
  Rubric box opens with: "Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem."
  That is the full voice budget for this section — do not add opinion inside the algebra.

[MID-POST CTA] ← WEIGHT: NEUTRAL
  Clean ask. Student button + parent italics line. No philosophy.

[WORKED EXAMPLE 2 — MCQ RECOGNITION TYPE — 400-500 words] ← WEIGHT: PROCESS
  (for limit/form-recognition posts) or [FRQ-LEVEL] for procedure-heavy posts
  See MCQ vs. FRQ framing rules in Section 4.
  Rubric box opens with: "Here is exactly what the AP exam is checking on this problem type."
  Same budget as Example 1: intro + process + coaching close only.

[THE 3 MISTAKES — 150-200 words] ← WEIGHT: MEDIUM
  Each mistake gets: 1 WHY sentence (coaching, short) + 1 consequence line.
  That is the full budget per mistake. No additional opinion layers.
  Real numbers for the consequence (points lost, exam context).
  Triage rule: if the post has a time-pressure angle (exam in 2 weeks, cram scenario), add one sentence per mistake flagging "fix now" or "accept and move on." Be honest — some mistakes are not worth fixing with 2 weeks left.

[PRACTICE PROBLEMS — 3 problems] ← WEIGHT: LIGHT
  Coaching opener only ("Try these before checking answers...").
  Problems and answers are neutral. No stances inside answers.

[FAQ — 3-4 questions] ← WEIGHT: LIGHT + ZACH CLOSE
  Each answer: direct and factual first. No philosophizing inside the answer.
  Each answer closes with one Zach line (coaching, consequence, or connection to next unit).

[QUICK REFERENCE BOX] — "Save This For Exam Day" ← WEIGHT: NEUTRAL
  Just the reference. Real notation, real numbers. No stance.

[RELATED POSTS] ← WEIGHT: NEUTRAL
[END CTA + SEASONAL CTA if applicable] ← WEIGHT: NEUTRAL + 1 consequence line on end CTA only
[SEO METADATA]
[ZACH'S FLOW CHECKLIST]
```

## 7. MATHJAX FORMATTING

- Inline math: `$f(x) = x^2$`
- Display math: `$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]$$` on its own line
- Fractions: `\frac{numerator}{denominator}`
- Derivatives: `\frac{dy}{dx}` or `f'(x)`
- Integrals: `\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx`
- Limits: `\lim_{x \to a} f(x)`
- Square roots: `\sqrt{x}`
- Boxed answers: `\boxed{f'(x) = 2x}`

## 12. AUDIT SPLIT — MECHANICAL VS. SUBJECTIVE

The audit happens in two separate passes. Drafters do not need to self-audit the mechanical layer — the `mechanical-auditor` sub-agent runs after every draft and will reject the post if any deterministic rule fails. Drafters should still write to the spirit of the rules (it costs less to write it right than to retry), but the canonical gate is the auditor.

### Mechanical pass (sub-agent — automatic)
Deterministic only. Pure regex / count / structure checks. No voice judgment.
- Zero em-dashes
- Zero forbidden phrases (Section 2)
- No section opens with "Definition:" or "The [X] is..."
- All MathJax `$...$` and `$$...$$` delimiters balanced
- AROC vs. IROC distinction surfaced if rate / velocity / slope / tangent appears
- MCQ-vs-FRQ rubric opener present per Section 4 rules
- All required structural sections from Section 5 present
- Word count in [3000, 4500]
- Seasonal CTA gating correct for the post's date (Jan 1 – Sep 15 only for summer programs)

If any check fails, the auditor returns the failure list with line numbers and the drafter rewrites the failing sections. Up to 2 retry loops before escalating to Zach.

### Subjective pass (Zach — manual, ~6 min/post)
Voice judgment only. Layers 1, 2, 4 from Section 3 — opinion injection, contradiction move, ugly truth. Layer 3 (real numbers) and Layer 5 (voice/readability) are partially covered by the mechanical pass; spot-check them but do not full-score.

Read the `.html` preview cold. For each major section, ask:
- Does Zach have a *take* here, or is this just the textbook in better clothes?
- Is there a contradiction or a "you would think... it isn't" move where one would land?
- Is the ugly truth named — the cost of skipping this, the gap most students have?

If yes → ship. If no → flag the section, return to drafter with a one-line voice note.

### Drafter responsibility
Drafters should produce a post that passes the mechanical pass on the first try and gives Zach minimum surface area for subjective rejection. Skipping the mechanical rules to "save time" wastes more time on retry. Skipping voice depth wastes Zach's audit attention.

## 14. OPENING HOOK FORMATS + 4-POST ROTATION

Every post begins with one of 4 hook types. The drafter reads `HOOK_TYPE` from the topic block and writes the first Intro sentence to match. Override is allowed but must be justified in the SKILL DELTA block.

### 4-post rotation
Tracked in `content-queue.md` as `HOOK_ROTATION_POSITION` (1-4) and `NEXT_HOOK_TYPE`. The rotation advances by 1 each time a post is archived via `/blog-archive`. After position 4, wrap back to 1.

Rotation order:
1. `counterintuitive-stat`
2. `student-moment`
3. `uncomfortable-truth`
4. `name-the-feeling`

### The 4 hook types

**`counterintuitive-stat`**
Lead with a real number that reframes the challenge. The stat must be accurate — never fabricated or rounded dramatically.
- Structure: stat → why that changes everything → what the student should do differently
- Use when: post is about test strategy, scoring, or study planning
- ACE example: "You only need about 60% of available points to score a 5 on AP Calculus AB. Most students are studying like they need 90%."

**`student-moment`**
Name exactly what the student is doing or feeling right now. Specific to the season if possible.
- Structure: this is your moment → here is why it matters → here is what you are about to learn
- Use when: post is time-sensitive, season-relevant, or addresses a specific exam phase
- ACE example: "It's April and you're staring at your composite score wondering if a 5 is still possible. It is. But only if you stop reviewing everything and start targeting the right things."

**`uncomfortable-truth`**
The thing other blogs won't say. State it plainly. Then immediately give the plan.
- Structure: the truth → why it is true → the path forward
- Use when: post addresses a common student mistake or a gap most review materials ignore
- ACE example: "If it's April and you haven't done a single timed FRQ, you're behind. Not a little behind. The kind of behind that requires a specific plan, not more flashcard reviews."

**`name-the-feeling`**
Give a name to an unnamed student experience. Students recognize themselves and keep reading.
- Structure: name the phenomenon → describe it precisely → show why it matters for this topic
- Use when: post addresses a psychological or strategic student behavior pattern
- ACE example: "Every AP Calculus student hits what I call the Confidence Trap: three weeks of solid practice, then a full-length test, and the score barely moves. The problem is not effort. It is practice type."

### Hook comment format (required in every draft)
```
<!-- HOOK TYPE: counterintuitive-stat -->
[First sentence of intro here]
```

The comment goes on the line immediately before the first sentence of the Intro. The mechanical-auditor checks for this comment.

### Screenshot-able one-liner (required in every post)
Every post must contain one sentence under 20 words that a student could screenshot and text to a friend. Requirements:
- Standalone — makes sense without surrounding context
- Not a platitude
- Under 20 words

Mark it in the draft:
```
<!-- SCREENSHOT-ABLE ONE-LINER -->
[sentence here]
<!-- END ONE-LINER -->
```

The mechanical-auditor checks that both comments are present.

## 15. SEO ARCHITECTURE PRINCIPLES

These principles apply at the structural level, not just in metadata. The drafter applies them. Zach's subjective audit pass verifies them.

**Principle 1 — Exam anatomy before strategy.**
Always orient the reader with format, scoring, and timing before giving study tips or technique advice. A student who does not know the FRQ count and time limits cannot effectively use strategy advice. Mandatory for `ultimate-guide` and `named-plan`. For other types, include at least one orientation paragraph if the post makes strategy claims.

**Principle 2 — Big Ideas framework.**
When a post covers multiple AP Calculus topics, organize content using College Board's Big Ideas structure: Limits → Derivatives → Integrals (AB), plus Series for BC. Reference the Big Ideas by name when they are relevant. Do not invent a custom organization when the College Board framework already gives students the map they see in class.

**Principle 3 — Tiered priority labeling.**
For posts covering multiple concepts, label every concept by exam frequency:
- **ESSENTIAL** — tested every single year, often multiple times
- **IMPORTANT** — tested most years
- **SUPPLEMENTARY** — tested occasionally, worth knowing if time allows

Be honest. A Rapid Review where every concept is labeled ESSENTIAL is not a triage tool. Required for `rapid-review` and `ultimate-guide`.

**Principle 4 — Score-to-outcome connections.**
When score levels are relevant, connect them to real college credit outcomes. Not just "a 5 is the top score" — but what credit that score typically earns. Example: "A 5 on AP Calc BC typically earns 8-10 semester credit hours at most universities, placing you directly into Calculus III or differential equations." Claims must be accurate — verify against College Board's AP credit policy database if needed.

**Principle 5 — FRQ communication training.**
Posts covering FRQ-type content must address justifying reasoning, not just solving. Students lose partial credit because their notation is incomplete or their reasoning is unstated, not because their math is wrong. At least one worked example in an FRQ post must include explicit coaching on the communication layer: what to write, not just what to compute. Example: "You must write 'by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus' before the conclusion line, or the justification step earns zero even if your integral is correct."

## 16. PILLAR CLUSTER ARCHITECTURE

Every AP Calculus unit maps to one pillar post (the authoritative `ultimate-guide` for that unit) and multiple spoke posts that link back to it. This structure compounds SEO value over time.

### Cluster roles

**Pillar** — the everything-you-need resource for one unit. One per unit. Always `post_type: ultimate-guide`. The pillar's Related Posts section lists all published spokes for its unit (updated manually by Zach when a new spoke ships).

**Spoke** — a narrower post within a unit. Points to its pillar with at least one internal link. Can be any post type.

### Topic block fields for cluster

```
CLUSTER_ROLE: pillar | spoke
PILLAR_SLUG: <slug of the unit's pillar post> (required when CLUSTER_ROLE: spoke)
```

The mechanical-auditor (check 21) confirms that every spoke contains a link to its declared `PILLAR_SLUG`.

### Linking rules

- Every spoke must contain at least one internal link to its `PILLAR_SLUG`. Natural anchor text — not "click here."
- The pillar's Related Posts section must list all published spokes for its unit. Zach updates this manually when a spoke ships.
- If the pillar for a unit has not yet been written, set `CLUSTER_ROLE: spoke` and `PILLAR_SLUG: TBD`. The auditor will skip check 21 when `PILLAR_SLUG` is "TBD."

## 17. ANNUAL DATA FRESHNESS PROTOCOL

### What this covers
Any post containing exam-specific data must declare `DATA_YEAR: YYYY` in its YAML front matter. This is the year the data was verified against College Board's official materials.

**What counts as exam data:**
- AP score cut scores (composite thresholds for 3, 4, 5)
- MCQ and FRQ question counts and time limits
- Calculator-active vs. calculator-inactive section breakdown
- Scoring weights (percent of exam score per section)
- College credit examples ("a 5 typically earns X credit hours at...")

### Freshness rule
If `DATA_YEAR` is more than 12 months older than the post's `DATE`, the mechanical-auditor emits a WARNING (not a FAIL). The data may still be accurate — the warning is a trigger for Zach to verify before publishing.

### Annual review
Every May (after the current year's AP exam), run `/blog-refresh` on all posts with stale `DATA_YEAR`. Update changed values, advance `DATA_YEAR` to the current year, re-run the auditor, and republish via WordPress.
