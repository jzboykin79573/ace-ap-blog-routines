The Starting-Line Problem

There is a specific feeling that hits when you read "find a particular solution" on the FRQ. You know integration is somewhere in the answer. You can feel it. But you cannot find the starting line.

That is exactly what happened to students in both of my groups on the May 5 mock exam. The sub-part asked them to separate variables and find a particular solution given initial conditions. I watched the same thing play out in two separate sessions: students who understood the concept, who knew integration was involved, who had done similar problems in practice. And still could not write the first line.

Here is what that first line looks like:

$$\frac{dH}{\sqrt{4+H}} = 12\,dT$$

That is it. That is the separation. Students who wrote that line got most of the remaining 5 points in that sub-part. Students who did not left 5 on the table.

On the May 5 mock, that separation sub-part was worth 5 out of 9 total points on that FRQ. It was the single most missed section across both groups. Not because the math was impossible. Because students could not write the first line.

This post fixes that. I am going to walk you through the full setup: separate, u-substitution, integrate, apply initial conditions, solve for C, isolate H. Every step. No gaps. And when you are done reading, that first line will never be the problem again.

This is the skill that separates students who score 4s and 5s on FRQs from students who leave partial-credit points scattered across every question.

What Separable Differential Equations Actually Mean

Most students learn about separable differential equations in AP Calculus as a procedure. Separate the variables. Integrate both sides. Solve for C. That is true. But if that is all you know, you will get tripped up the moment the equation does not look like the textbook example.

Here is the analogy I use. Think of a differential equation as two people in a room who are not allowed to be on the same side of an equals sign. A separable equation is one where you can move every piece involving $y$ (or $H$, or whatever dependent variable you have) to the left side, and every piece involving $x$ (or $T$) to the right side. Separated. No mixing allowed once you have done it.

The formal definition: a separable differential equation is one that can be written in the form

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x) \cdot g(y)$$

where $f(x)$ is a function of $x$ only and $g(y)$ is a function of $y$ only. Once you have that form, divide both sides by $g(y)$, multiply both sides by $dx$, and the variables are separated. Then integrate.

The reason this matters for the AP exam: most FRQ differential equation sub-parts are testing exactly this skill. You are given a differential equation and asked to find a particular solution. Separation of variables is the mechanism. It is how solving differential equations by hand starts in Unit 7.

One distinction worth naming here: a differential equation describes an instantaneous rate of change. How fast is $H$ changing at this exact moment as $T$ changes? That is the IROC framing. It is not an average over an interval. The AROC would describe how much $H$ changed across a whole interval of time. The differential equation only captures the instantaneous picture at each specific point on the curve. That is why initial conditions let you pin down one specific solution curve out of the infinitely many curves the general equation describes.

The one thing I need you to understand before we go to the steps: this is not about recognizing that integration is in there. You already know that. The question is whether you can write the separated form first. That is the gatekeeping step. That is what the AP exam is actually testing when it asks you to "find a particular solution to the differential equation."

The 4-Step Separation Framework

Every separable differential equation problem on the AP exam runs through the same 4 steps. Write these on your scratch paper at the start of every FRQ that involves a differential equation, before you even read the specific question.

1
Separate the variables

Get all $y$ terms (and $dy$) on one side. Get all $x$ terms (and $dx$) on the other. Write the differential notation explicitly. Do not leave $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a fraction you are going to work around. Move it.

$$g(y)\,dy = f(x)\,dx$$
2
Integrate both sides

Put an integral sign on each side. Write $+C$ immediately on the right after integrating. Do not skip it. If either side requires a u-substitution, do that work now and show it explicitly.

$$\int g(y)\,dy = \int f(x)\,dx + C$$
3
Apply the initial condition

Substitute the given values into your integrated equation. Solve for $C$. Write the value explicitly. Never assume $C = 0$.

4
Isolate the dependent variable (if asked)

Some FRQ parts ask for an explicit solution $y = f(x)$. Others accept implicit form. Read the question. If it says "find $H$ in terms of $T$," isolate $H$. When in doubt, isolate. You cannot lose points for going further. You can lose points for stopping early.

Process note: Write $+C$ on one side after integrating and leave it there until you solve for it using the initial condition. If you write $+C_1$ and $+C_2$ on both sides, that is fine. Immediately rename the combined constant $C = C_2 - C_1$. The AP rubric cares that $C$ is present and solved. It does not care which side you put it on.

Worked Example 1: The May 5 Mock Exam Problem

This is the exact FRQ sub-part from the May 5 mock exam. Both student groups saw this problem. Students who wrote the first separation line correctly got most of the points. Students who did not got zero on this sub-part.

The Problem

A container holds water. Let $H$ represent the height of the water in centimeters and $T$ represent time in minutes. The rate of change of height satisfies

$$\frac{dH}{dT} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{4+H}}$$

At time $T = 0$, the height is $H = 0$. Find the particular solution $H(T)$.

Step 1: Separate the variables

Move $\sqrt{4+H}$ to the left, $dT$ to the right.

$$\frac{dH}{\sqrt{4+H}} = 12\,dT \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (4+H)^{-1/2}\,dH = 12\,dT$$

This is the line. Write it. Once it is on the page, the rest follows directly.

Step 2: Integrate both sides

The right side: $\displaystyle\int 12\,dT = 12T$.

The left side requires a u-substitution. Write it explicitly:

$$\text{Let } u = 4+H, \quad du = dH$$ $$\int u^{-1/2}\,du = \frac{u^{1/2}}{1/2} = 2u^{1/2} = 2\sqrt{4+H}$$

After integrating both sides:

$$2\sqrt{4+H} = 12T + C$$

Step 3: Apply the initial condition $T = 0$, $H = 0$

$$2\sqrt{4+0} = 12(0) + C$$ $$2(2) = C \quad \Rightarrow \quad C = 4$$
$$2\sqrt{4+H} = 12T + 4$$

Step 4: Isolate $H$

$$\sqrt{4+H} = 6T + 2$$ $$4+H = (6T+2)^2$$ $$\boxed{H(T) = (6T+2)^2 - 4}$$
Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Correctly separated variables: $dH$ and $\sqrt{4+H}$ on the left, $12\,dT$ on the right: 1 point
  • Correct antiderivative on the left, including the u-substitution: 1 point
  • $+C$ present after integration: 1 point
  • Initial condition substituted correctly and $C = 4$ found: 1 point
  • Correct explicit particular solution $H(T) = (6T+2)^2 - 4$ stated: 1 point
FRQ communication rule: Write your u-substitution work explicitly: "Let $u = 4+H$, $du = dH$" before computing the antiderivative. Even if you get the right answer, missing this shown work can cost you the integration point. One line. Protects one point.

Worked Example 2: A Different FRQ Setup

Same 4 steps. Different integration technique on the left side. This is the kind of variety the AP exam uses to check whether you actually understand the procedure or just memorized one example.

The Problem

Solve the separable differential equation

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 e^{-y}$$

with the initial condition $y(0) = 1$.

Step 1: Separate the variables

$$e^y\,dy = 3x^2\,dx$$

Step 2: Integrate both sides

$$\int e^y\,dy = \int 3x^2\,dx$$ $$e^y = x^3 + C$$

Step 3: Apply the initial condition $x = 0$, $y = 1$

$$e^1 = 0^3 + C \quad \Rightarrow \quad C = e$$ $$e^y = x^3 + e$$

Step 4: Isolate $y$

$$\boxed{y = \ln(x^3 + e)}$$
Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Correct separation: $e^y\,dy$ on one side, $3x^2\,dx$ on the other: 1 point
  • Correct antiderivatives on both sides: 1 point
  • $+C$ present and on the correct side: 1 point
  • Initial condition applied correctly and $C = e$ found: 1 point
  • Explicit solution $y = \ln(x^3 + e)$ clearly stated: 1 point
Sign check: If you land a negative number inside a natural log, go back and check your integration signs and initial condition substitution first. $\ln$ of a negative number is undefined. It means the algebra went wrong somewhere, not that the answer is valid.

The 3 Mistakes That Cost Points on This Sub-Part

Mistake 1: Seeing integration but not writing the separation first
Why it happens: Every student knows integration is involved. So they try to integrate $\frac{dH}{dT} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{4+H}}$ directly, as if $H$ were already isolated. The right side contains $H$, not just $T$. You cannot integrate it with respect to $T$ while $H$ is sitting in it.

The fix: Before you write a single integral sign, write the separated form. Full stop. Step 1 exists for a reason.

Consequence: On the May 5 mock exam, students who skipped this step forfeited up to 4 of the 5 available points on that sub-part.
Mistake 2: Leaving the left side unintegrated (forgetting the u-substitution)
Why it happens: The integral $\int (4+H)^{-1/2}\,dH$ does not look standard at first glance. Students try to write $\ln\sqrt{4+H}$ instead of applying the power rule with the correct substitution, or they stall entirely.

The fix: Any composite expression inside a power, like $(4+H)^{-1/2}$ or $\sqrt{something + variable}$, is a u-substitution trigger. Set $u$ equal to the inner expression. Write it. One line of scratch work protects the integration point.

Consequence: Even with correct separation, a wrong antiderivative costs the integration point and contaminates the $C$ calculation. One missed technique, two lost points.
Mistake 3: Forgetting to write $+C$ before applying the initial condition
Why it happens: Students complete the integration, get $2\sqrt{4+H} = 12T$, and substitute the initial condition to verify. But $+C$ was never written. They assume $C = 0$ because the equation "balanced." Except $2\sqrt{4} = 4$, not $0$. $C = 4$, and they lost it.

The fix: $+C$ goes in immediately after integration, before anything else. Write it. Solve for it using the initial condition. Never assume it is zero.

Consequence: On a 9-point FRQ, losing both the $C$ point and the particular solution point is a 2-point hit from one omission.

Try these on your own before checking the answers. If you get stuck on the separation step, write the equation in the form $g(y)\,dy = f(x)\,dx$ before you reach for the integral sign.

Problem 1: Direct Separation
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy, \quad y(0) = 3$$

Find the particular solution.

Problem 2: Requires u-Substitution on One Side
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4x}{(1+x^2)^2}, \quad y(0) = 2$$

Find the particular solution.

Problem 3: Word Problem Setup

A population $P$ (in thousands) grows according to $\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 0.5P$. At time $t = 0$, the population is $P = 8$ thousand. Write a particular solution $P(t)$ and state the population at $t = 4$.

Answers
Problem 1: Separate: $\frac{dy}{y} = 2x\,dx$. Integrate: $\ln|y| = x^2 + C$. Apply $y(0) = 3$: $\ln 3 = C$. Particular solution: $y = 3e^{x^2}$.
Problem 2: Separate: $dy = \frac{4x}{(1+x^2)^2}\,dx$. Let $u = 1+x^2$, $du = 2x\,dx$. Right side: $\int \frac{2}{u^2}\,du = -\frac{2}{u} = -\frac{2}{1+x^2}$. Apply $y(0) = 2$: $2 = -2 + C$, so $C = 4$. Particular solution: $y = 4 - \dfrac{2}{1+x^2}$.
Problem 3: Separate: $\frac{dP}{P} = 0.5\,dt$. Integrate: $\ln|P| = 0.5t + C$. Apply $P(0) = 8$: $C = \ln 8$. Particular solution: $P(t) = 8e^{0.5t}$. At $t = 4$: $P(4) = 8e^{2} \approx 59.1$ thousand.

FAQ

Q: What makes a differential equation "separable"?+

A differential equation is separable if you can rewrite it so that all the $y$ terms (including $dy$) are on one side and all the $x$ terms (including $dx$) are on the other. The formal test: can you write $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a product $f(x) \cdot g(y)$? If yes, divide by $g(y)$, multiply by $dx$, and you have separated it. If the equation mixes $x$ and $y$ in a way that does not factor, it is not separable and you need a different method.

For Unit 7 on the AP exam, if you are asked to "find a particular solution to the differential equation," it is almost always separable. That is what the FRQ sub-part is testing.

Q: How do I solve separable differential equations step by step on the AP exam?+

Four steps, every time: (1) separate, $g(y)\,dy = f(x)\,dx$; (2) integrate both sides and write $+C$ immediately; (3) apply the initial condition and solve for $C$; (4) isolate the dependent variable if the problem asks for explicit form. Do not skip Step 1. Do not skip $+C$. Those are the two most common single points lost on the AP FRQ.

The AP Calculus FRQ rubric for separation of variables problems typically allocates 1 point per step, so every step matters independently. You can earn 3 out of 5 points even if you do not finish. But only if you show the steps. For a pure math reference on the technique, see Paul's Online Notes on separation of variables.

Q: Is separation of variables the same as integration?+

No. Separation comes before integration. Separation is the algebraic step that gets the variables on opposite sides. Integration is what you do after separation. Students who confuse the order try to integrate the original equation without separating first. They cannot, because both variables are mixed together on one side.

Think of it this way: separation makes the integrals possible. Without it, you are trying to integrate $\frac{12}{\sqrt{4+H}}$ with respect to $T$, and $H$ is floating there with no mechanism to handle it. Separation removes that problem. Then integration runs normally.

Q: Do I always need an explicit solution on the AP FRQ?+

Read the question carefully. "Find a particular solution" usually means explicit form: $y = f(x)$ or $H = f(T)$. "Solve the differential equation" without specifying explicit sometimes accepts implicit form with $C$ solved. When in doubt, isolate.

In the May 5 mock problem, the question said "find the particular solution $H(T)$." Explicit was required. Stopping at $2\sqrt{4+H} = 12T + 4$ without isolating $H$ would likely lose the final point. One more step, one more point. Do not stop early.
