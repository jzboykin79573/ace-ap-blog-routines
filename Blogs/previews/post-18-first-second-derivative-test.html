The Critical Point Trap

A student in my class last spring could find every derivative on the unit test and still lost 9 points on the free response because she found the critical points and stopped there, never checking whether they were actually a max, a min, or neither.

I see this exact pattern every year. The student does the hard part right. They set up $f'(x)$, factor it, solve for the zeros. Critical points: found. Then they circle those x-values and move on. The FRQ rubric has 5 more points sitting there waiting for them. And they left every one of those points on the table.

Here is the thing about critical points on the first and second derivative test AP Calculus problems: they are not the answer. They are the starting line. The AP exam is scoring the classification, not the algebra that finds the point. You need to identify which critical points are local maxima, which are local minima, and which are neither. And you need to justify your answer in notation the AP grader can read.

Finding critical points is not the skill. Classifying them is.

That gap costs students 6 to 9 points on Unit 5 FRQs every single year. Not because the classification is hard once you know the procedure. Because most students treat finding critical points as the finish line when it is the 100-meter mark in a 400-meter race.

This post gives you both methods, both frameworks, a comparison that will save you from a specific AP exam trap, and every rubric point the grader is looking for.

What the Two Tests Are Actually Answering

Here is the framing I give every student before we start Unit 5.

The First Derivative Test and the Second Derivative Test answer the exact same question: at this critical point, is the function experiencing a local max, a local min, or neither? Two methods. Same question. Different tools.

Most students think of them as unrelated topics because the textbook puts them in separate sections. They are not. They are two roads to the same destination. If you know both, you can switch between them. If you only know one, you are stuck when the other would be faster or the one you chose turns out to be inconclusive.

Quick reminder before we build the frameworks. Every critical point in this unit is about the instantaneous behavior of the function at a specific x-value. That is the IROC framing. If the AP exam gives you a table and asks for the average rate of change between $x = 0$ and $x = 2$, that is not what we are doing here. Average rate of change (AROC) uses two points on an interval. Instantaneous rate of change (IROC) is the derivative at one specific point. The derivative tests classify behavior at one point. Stay in IROC territory.

Average Rate of Change (AROC)
$\Delta y / \Delta x$
Slope between two points on an interval. Not what the derivative tests use.
Interval-based, not this unit
Instantaneous Rate of Change (IROC)
$f'(c)$
Slope of the tangent line at one specific point $c$. This is the derivative test input.
This is what we classify

A critical point of $f$ is any value $c$ in the domain where $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist. The derivative being zero means a horizontal tangent line at $x = c$. The derivative not existing means a corner, cusp, or vertical tangent. Both are critical points. Both need classification.

You would think that any point where $f'(c) = 0$ must be a local max or min. It is not. $f(x) = x^3$ has $f'(0) = 0$. The critical point at $x = 0$ is neither a max nor a min. It is an inflection point with a horizontal tangent. This is exactly why classifying the critical point matters. Finding it tells you where to look. Classifying it tells you what you are actually looking at.

The First Derivative Test: Sign Chart Framework

The First Derivative Test uses the sign of $f'(x)$ on intervals around the critical point to classify it. The logic: if $f'(x) > 0$ on an interval, $f$ is increasing there. If $f'(x) < 0$, $f$ is decreasing. A local max happens where the function stops increasing and starts decreasing. A local min happens where it stops decreasing and starts increasing.

1
Find all critical points

Solve $f'(x) = 0$ and identify any x-values where $f'$ is undefined but $f$ itself is defined.

2
Place critical points on a number line

The critical points divide the real line into intervals. Each interval gets its own sign test.

3
Pick a test value in each interval, evaluate $f'$, record the sign

You do not need the exact value. Positive or negative is all you need.

4
Read the sign pattern at each critical point

Positive → Negative: local maximum.
Negative → Positive: local minimum.
No sign change: neither (no extremum).

5
Read increasing/decreasing intervals from the same chart

Where $f' > 0$: $f$ is increasing. Where $f' < 0$: $f$ is decreasing. You get both classifications from one sign chart. Free.

FRQ communication rule: A sign chart alone does not earn the justification point. After the chart, write the sentence: "$f'(x)$ changes from negative to positive at $x = 2$, so $f$ has a local minimum at $x = 2$." That sentence is often a dedicated rubric point. Missing it is a free miss.

Worked Example 1: FDT on $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$ (Foundational)

We are going to use $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$ as the primary function throughout this post. Critical points at $x = 0$ and $x = 2$. Watch how the sign chart classifies both.

The Problem

For $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$, find all local maxima and minima using the First Derivative Test. State all intervals on which $f$ is increasing and decreasing.

Step 1: Find $f'(x)$

$$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x-2)$$

Step 2: Find critical points

$$3x(x-2) = 0 \implies x = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad x = 2$$

Both are in the domain of $f$. Both are critical points. Both need classification.

Step 3: Build the sign chart

Three intervals: $(-\infty, 0)$, $(0, 2)$, and $(2, \infty)$.

Interval Test value $f'$(test) Sign of $f'$ $f$ behavior
$(-\infty, 0)$ $x = -1$ $3(-1)(-3) = 9$ Positive (+) Increasing
$(0, 2)$ $x = 1$ $3(1)(-1) = -3$ Negative (−) Decreasing
$(2, \infty)$ $x = 3$ $3(3)(1) = 9$ Positive (+) Increasing

Step 4: Classify each critical point

At $x = 0$: $f'$ changes from positive to negative. Local maximum.

$$f(0) = (0)^3 - 3(0)^2 + 2 = 2 \implies \text{local max at } (0,\, 2)$$

At $x = 2$: $f'$ changes from negative to positive. Local minimum.

$$f(2) = (2)^3 - 3(2)^2 + 2 = 8 - 12 + 2 = -2 \implies \text{local min at } (2,\, {-2})$$

Step 5: Increasing/Decreasing Intervals

Increasing on $(-\infty, 0)$ and $(2, \infty)$. Decreasing on $(0, 2)$.

Live Graph: $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$ | local max at $(0, 2)$, local min at $(2, -2)$
Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Find $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x$ correctly: 1 point
  • Identify both critical points $x = 0$ and $x = 2$: 1 point
  • Construct a valid sign chart with correct signs in each interval: 1 point
  • Classify $x = 0$ as local max with written justification (sign changes positive to negative): 1 point
  • Classify $x = 2$ as local min with written justification (sign changes negative to positive): 1 point
  • State $f(0) = 2$ and $f(2) = -2$ when asked for the max/min values: 1 point

That is 6 points. Every point is earned by something you wrote after the critical points. The critical points themselves earn exactly 1 of those 6.

Worked Example 2: The Second Derivative Test and When It Fails

The Second Derivative Test is faster than the FDT for most functions. It has a case where it completely fails. The AP exam knows this and uses it to separate scores.

The SDT Setup

If $c$ is a critical point where $f'(c) = 0$, evaluate $f''(c)$:

  • $f''(c) < 0$: local maximum at $x = c$ (concave down at the peak)
  • $f''(c) > 0$: local minimum at $x = c$ (concave up at the bottom)
  • $f''(c) = 0$: inconclusive. Fall back to FDT immediately.

That third case is the AP exam trap. Saying "inconclusive" and stopping gets you zero. Recognizing that FDT is the backup gets you the points.

Back to $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$

$$f''(x) = 6x - 6$$

At $x = 0$: $f''(0) = 6(0) - 6 = -6 < 0$ → local maximum. Confirmed.

At $x = 2$: $f''(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6 > 0$ → local minimum. Confirmed.

Same answer as FDT. Two methods, same result. The SDT is faster here because we only plugged in two numbers.

Now the Inconclusive Case: $g(x) = x^4$

The Problem

Find any local extrema of $g(x) = x^4$. Apply the SDT first, then handle the inconclusive result.

Step 1: Find $g'(x)$ and the critical point

$$g'(x) = 4x^3 = 0 \implies x = 0$$

Step 2: Attempt the SDT

$$g''(x) = 12x^2 \implies g''(0) = 12(0)^2 = 0$$

SDT is inconclusive. We cannot conclude anything from this alone. Pivoting to FDT.

Step 3: Apply FDT to $g(x) = x^4$

IntervalTest value$g'$(test)Sign
$(-\infty, 0)$ $x = -1$ $4(-1)^3 = -4$ Negative
$(0, \infty)$ $x = 1$ $4(1)^3 = 4$ Positive

$g'$ changes from negative to positive at $x = 0$. Local minimum at $(0, 0)$.

The SDT gave us nothing. The FDT gave us the answer. This is why FDT is the backup you always carry into the AP exam.

Live Graph: $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x$ | sign behavior, zeros at $x = 0$ and $x = 2$
Here is exactly how the AP exam scores the SDT problem type
  • Compute $f''(x)$ correctly: 1 point
  • Evaluate $f''(c)$ at each critical point and state the sign: 1 point
  • Correctly classify local max or local min based on sign of $f''(c)$: 1 point
  • Recognize $f''(c) = 0$ and explicitly state SDT is inconclusive: 1 point
  • Apply FDT correctly after SDT fails, with sign chart: 1-2 points
  • State final classification with function value at the point: 1 point

On a 9-point FRQ, the inconclusive sub-part can be worth 3 points by itself. Students who write "SDT fails, therefore no conclusion" and stop lose all 3. Students who pivot to FDT earn all 3.

The SDT vs. the Concavity Test: These Are Not the Same Thing

You already know about concavity. The Second Derivative Test is not the Concavity Test. I need you to feel this distinction, not just memorize it.

Both tests use $f''(x)$. That is where the similarity ends. They answer completely different questions.

SDT vs. Concavity Test: Side by Side
Concavity Test

Input: an interval

Question: over this stretch of x-values, is $f$ concave up or concave down?

Method: check whether $f''(x) > 0$ or $f''(x) < 0$ across the interval

Output: a region: "concave up on $(0, 4)$"

Second Derivative Test

Input: one critical point $c$ where $f'(c) = 0$

Question: at this specific point, is there a local max or local min?

Method: evaluate $f''(c)$ at the single critical point

Output: a classification: "local min at $x = 2$"

The AP exam trap: A student finishes the SDT and writes "concave up on $(0, 4)$" as the answer. That is the Concavity Test answer smuggled into an SDT problem. The rubric awards no points for it.

Wrong answer: "$f$ is concave up on $(0, 4)$, so it's a minimum."
Right answer: "$f''(2) = 6 > 0$, so by the Second Derivative Test, $f$ has a local minimum at $x = 2$."

Both answers use $f''$. One gives a region. One gives a point classification. The rubric awards the point classification. Students who conflate these two tests answer the wrong question. I have watched this happen. It costs points every single time.

The 3 Mistakes That Cost My Students Points

Mistake 1: Finding critical points and treating that as the answer
Why it happens: the most algebraically intense part of these problems is finding where $f'(x) = 0$. Students finish that work, see the x-values, and stop. The FRQ rubric typically awards 1 point for finding critical points and 3 to 5 more for classifying them. Stopping at the critical points is stopping after 1 point.

The fix: after you find critical points, write the phrase "now classify using FDT" or "now classify using SDT" next to each one. That visual cue keeps you from closing your pencil too early.

Consequence: students who skip classification lose 4 to 6 points on problems they set up perfectly. They did the math. They left the points.
Mistake 2: Writing "local max" or "local min" without the required justification
Why it happens: students know the answer. They circled it on their sign chart. They assume the grader can see what they did. The rubric does not work that way. The AP exam requires the justification written out in derivative notation or words.

The fix: after every classification, write one sentence: "$f'$ changes from negative to positive at $x = 2$, so $f$ has a local minimum there." That sentence is often a dedicated rubric point.

Consequence: on a 9-point FRQ, missing the justification sentence is a free miss on a point you earned with correct math. That is the gap between a 4 and a 5 composite.
Mistake 3: Treating SDT inconclusive as a final answer
Why it happens: students learn "if $f''(c) = 0$, the SDT is inconclusive" as a fact and stop. Nobody told them what happens next.

The fix: when SDT fails, FDT is the fallback. Always. Write "SDT inconclusive at $x = c$, will use FDT." Then build the sign chart. The AP exam includes these problems specifically to find the students who know both tests.

Consequence: students who freeze at "inconclusive" lose 2 to 3 points. Students who pivot to FDT collect them all.

Problem 2: SDT Application

Let $p(x) = 2x^3 - 9x^2 + 12x - 4$. Use the Second Derivative Test to classify all critical points. If the SDT is inconclusive anywhere, fall back to FDT.

Problem 3: FRQ Type, Both Tests + Inconclusive Awareness

Let $f(x) = x^4 - 8x^2 + 7$.

(a) Find all critical points of $f$.

(b) Apply the SDT to each critical point. If the SDT is inconclusive, state so explicitly and classify using FDT.

(c) Identify all intervals on which $f$ is increasing and all intervals on which $f$ is decreasing.

Answers
Problem 1: $h'(x) = 3x^2 - 12 = 3(x-2)(x+2)$. Critical points at $x = -2$ and $x = 2$. Sign chart: positive on $(-\infty,-2)$, negative on $(-2,2)$, positive on $(2,\infty)$. Local maximum at $(-2, 16)$: $h'$ changes positive to negative. Local minimum at $(2, -16)$: $h'$ changes negative to positive. Increasing on $(-\infty,-2)$ and $(2,\infty)$. Decreasing on $(-2,2)$.
Problem 2: $p'(x) = 6x^2 - 18x + 12 = 6(x-1)(x-2)$. Critical points at $x = 1$ and $x = 2$. $p''(x) = 12x - 18$. $p''(1) = -6 < 0$: local maximum at $(1, 1)$. $p''(2) = 6 > 0$: local minimum at $(2, 0)$. SDT conclusive for both.
Problem 3: (a) $f'(x) = 4x^3 - 16x = 4x(x-2)(x+2)$. Critical points at $x = -2, 0, 2$.
(b) $f''(x) = 12x^2 - 16$. $f''(-2) = 32 > 0$: local min at $(-2, -9)$. $f''(0) = -16 < 0$: local max at $(0, 7)$. $f''(2) = 16 > 0$: local min at $(2, -9)$. SDT conclusive at all three.
(c) Sign chart of $f'$: increasing on $(-\infty,-2)$ and $(0,2)$. Decreasing on $(-2,0)$ and $(2,\infty)$.

FAQ

Q: When should I use the FDT vs. the SDT on the AP exam?+

Default to FDT when the problem does not specify a test, or when the function is complicated enough that computing $f''$ would take more time than a sign chart. Use SDT when the problem explicitly asks for it, or when $f''$ is easy to compute and you want the quickest classification path.

The most important rule: if $f''(c) = 0$ when you try SDT, stop and run FDT. Never leave "inconclusive" as your final answer. The AP exam is testing whether you know the backup exists.

Q: Do I need the function value $f(c)$ in my answer, or just the x-value?+

The AP rubric almost always wants both. "$f$ has a local minimum at $x = 2$" is partially correct. "$f$ has a local minimum at $(2, -2)$" is fully correct. When the FRQ asks "find the value of the local minimum," give both coordinates and the function value explicitly.

Read the question wording carefully. "At which x-value" means just $x$. "Find the local minimum value" means the y-value. One sentence in the prompt tells you exactly what the rubric is scoring.

Q: Can a function have a local max at a point where $f'$ does not exist?+

Yes. The FDT applies to any critical point, including corners and cusps where $f'$ is undefined. Build the sign chart with that point on the number line and check whether $f'$ changes sign. The SDT cannot be used at these points because $f''$ does not exist there either.

FDT only at corners and cusps. That is the rule. It comes up on both AB and BC exams in piecewise function problems.

Q: How does local max/min connect to absolute max/min problems in Unit 5?+

Local max/min from FDT and SDT are interior candidates. To find the absolute max/min on a closed interval $[a, b]$, evaluate $f$ at all local extrema inside the interval and at both endpoints. Then compare all the values. Absolute maximum is the largest. Absolute minimum is the smallest.

The derivative tests find your candidates. Endpoint evaluation completes the comparison. College Board tests both in the same FRQ problem regularly. Expect to see both in one question, and expect the rubric to award points for each step separately.
