The Invisible Theorem

There is a specific experience almost every AP Calculus student has with the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, and I call it the FTC Blind Spot. You have been using one part of it for months. You use it every single time you evaluate a definite integral. And then one day, the AP exam asks you to identify it by name, or to apply the other part, and your mind goes completely blank.

In a session on May 5, one of my students, Max, said exactly this: "I can't tell you what it is, but I know that I use it all the time without recognizing it." When I told him, "You know one of them for sure, because you use it every time you evaluate a definite integral," something clicked. The theorem he had been using automatically for weeks had never been named for him.

That is the exact gap the AP exam exploits. The fundamental theorem of calculus AP calculus questions are not asking you to discover something new. They are asking you to name something you already do, and then do the harder part, which most students have never practiced at all.

Here is the real cost. Two separate groups of my students missed FRQ Part C on the same exam because they did not know that $\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$, or that when the upper bound is $x^2$ instead of $x$, you need the chain rule. That is not a conceptual mystery. That is 3 to 4 points left on the table because one specific skill was never drilled.

This post names both parts, shows you exactly how to tell them apart, and gives you the chain rule extension that shows up on the AP exam every single year.

What the Two Parts Actually Mean

Most textbooks open this topic by calling one theorem "Part 1" and another "Part 2" and then listing definitions. I have never found that useful as a starting point. Here is the framing I use with every student instead.

Think about a thermostat in a room. The temperature has been changing all morning. You walk in and ask two different questions: "What is the current rate of temperature change?" And: "How much did the total temperature change from 8 a.m. to noon?"

Those are two completely different questions. One is about a rate at a specific moment. One is about accumulated change over an interval. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the bridge between those two questions. It says that integration and differentiation are inverse operations. Not similar operations. Inverse ones. That is not just a philosophical claim. It has two specific, testable forms, and the AP exam uses both.

FTC Part 1: The Derivative of an Integral

This is the part Max had never named. If you define a function by accumulating area under a curve from a fixed point $a$ to a variable point $x$, the derivative of that accumulation function is just the original integrand.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$$

You would think Part 1 is the simpler one because the result looks clean. It is not the part students use automatically. It is the one they blank on.

FTC Part 2: The Evaluation Theorem

This is what you have been doing every single time you compute a definite integral. You find an antiderivative $F(x)$ and evaluate $F(b) - F(a)$.

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

Every definite integral you have ever computed uses this. Every one. Most of my students learn this as "plug in the bounds" without ever connecting it to a theorem with a name. The AP exam will ask you to identify it or use it in a justification. Know both.

Naming note: Some older textbooks flip the numbering. What you see labeled "Part 2" in one curriculum may be called "Part 1" in another. The College Board's AP Calculus Course and Exam Description uses the evaluation theorem as Part 2. When in doubt, go by context: if the problem asks for a derivative of an integral, that is the differentiation form regardless of what your textbook calls it.

How to Tell Them Apart Every Time

The whole distinction comes down to one question: what is the problem asking you to do?

1
What is the operation being performed?

If the problem asks you to evaluate a definite integral and get a number: use FTC Part 2 (Evaluation Theorem).

If the problem gives you a function defined as an integral and asks for its derivative: use FTC Part 1 (Derivative of an Integral).

2
What does the expression look like?
You see this form...Use this
$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = ?$ (find the value) FTC Part 2: find antiderivative, evaluate $F(b)-F(a)$
$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = ?$ (upper bound is $x$) FTC Part 1: answer is just $f(x)$
$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{g(x)} f(t)\,dt = ?$ (upper bound is a function) FTC Part 1 + Chain Rule: $f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$
3
Watch the upper bound.

If the upper bound is $x$, the answer is just $f(x)$. If it is $x^2$, $\sin(x)$, or any other function of $x$, you apply the chain rule. The AP exam tests this specific variation every year.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{x^2} f(t)\,dt = f(x^2) \cdot 2x$$
AROC vs. IROC connection: FTC Part 2 is about accumulated change over an interval, which connects to average rate of change (AROC). FTC Part 1 produces an instantaneous rate at a specific point (IROC). If the exam asks for the average rate of change of $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$ over $[c,d]$, that is $\frac{F(d)-F(c)}{d-c}$. If it asks for the instantaneous rate at $x = c$, that is $F'(c) = f(c)$ by Part 1. Different question. Different answer. Different theorem.

Worked Example 1: FTC Part 2, Evaluating a Definite Integral

This is the version you already use. Work through it explicitly labeling each step because on FRQ Part C, the AP exam expects you to justify your work by name.

Problem: Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^4 (3x^2 - 2x + 5)\,dx$.

1
Identify the theorem.

We are evaluating a definite integral to get a number. This is FTC Part 2: $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$.

2
Find the antiderivative.
$$F(x) = x^3 - x^2 + 5x$$

Antiderivative of $3x^2$ is $x^3$. Of $-2x$ is $-x^2$. Of $5$ is $5x$. No $+C$ needed for definite integrals. It cancels in $F(b)-F(a)$.

3
Evaluate at the bounds.
$$F(4) = (4)^3 - (4)^2 + 5(4) = 64 - 16 + 20 = 68$$
$$F(1) = (1)^3 - (1)^2 + 5(1) = 1 - 1 + 5 = 5$$
4
Subtract.
$$\int_1^4 (3x^2 - 2x + 5)\,dx = F(4) - F(1) = 68 - 5 = \boxed{63}$$
Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Correct antiderivative with all three terms (no $+C$ required for definite integrals, but do not write one inconsistently)
  • Correct evaluation of $F(b)$ and $F(a)$ separately. The AP exam credits each computation even if you make an arithmetic error on the subtraction.
  • Correct subtraction $F(b) - F(a)$ in the right order. Writing $F(a) - F(b)$ is wrong and scores zero for that step.
  • On an FRQ, writing "by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2" before the antiderivative step earns the justification point. Skipping that line costs you the point even if your math is perfectly correct.

Worked Example 2: FTC Part 1 with Chain Rule (The MCQ Type That Trips Everyone)

This is the problem type that cost Max and two full groups of my students FRQ points. The AP exam gives you a function defined as an integral and asks for its derivative. If you have never drilled this form specifically, you will freeze.

Problem 1 (basic): Upper bound is plain $x$

Let $G(x) = \displaystyle\int_2^x (t^3 + \cos t)\,dt$. Find $G'(x)$.

Solution: Upper bound is $x$. Straight FTC Part 1. No chain rule needed.

$$G'(x) = x^3 + \cos x$$

Whatever was under the integral sign, written in terms of $x$ instead of $t$. That is the whole result.

Problem 2 (chain rule version): Upper bound is $x^2$

Let $H(x) = \displaystyle\int_0^{x^2} \sqrt{1 + t^3}\,dt$. Find $H'(x)$.

1

Upper bound is $x^2$, not $x$. Chain rule required.

2

Let $u = x^2$. By FTC Part 1: $\frac{dH}{du} = \sqrt{1+u^3}$.

3

Chain rule: $\frac{dH}{dx} = \frac{dH}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = \sqrt{1+u^3} \cdot 2x$.

4

Substitute $u = x^2$ back:

$$H'(x) = \sqrt{1+(x^2)^3} \cdot 2x = \boxed{2x\sqrt{1+x^6}}$$

Problem 3 (AP exam style): Variable in the lower bound

Let $F(x) = \displaystyle\int_x^5 e^{t^2}\,dt$. Find $F'(x)$.

Twist: the variable is in the lower bound, not the upper bound. Flip the bounds and flip the sign.

$$F(x) = -\int_5^x e^{t^2}\,dt$$
$$F'(x) = \boxed{-e^{x^2}}$$

Graph of $G(x) = \int_0^x (t^2+1)\,dt = \frac{x^3}{3}+x$ (navy). Its derivative $G'(x) = f(x) = x^2+1$ (gold). At $x=2$: $G'(2) = f(2) = 5$.

Here is exactly what the AP exam is checking on this problem type
  • Recognizing that a function defined as an integral with a variable upper bound calls for FTC Part 1
  • Applying the chain rule when the upper bound is a composite function: $x^2$, $\sin x$, $3x+1$
  • Handling a variable in the lower bound by flipping and negating before applying Part 1
  • Writing the answer in terms of $x$, not $t$. The $t$ is the dummy integration variable. It disappears in the result. If $t$ appears in your final answer, something went wrong.
  • On MCQ, this entire problem should take under 90 seconds. Pattern recognition is the skill.

The 3 Mistakes That Cost My Students Points

Mistake 1: Treating both parts as the same thing and picking one randomly
Why it happens: Students see "Fundamental Theorem of Calculus" and know they are supposed to use it, but have never cleanly separated the two parts. Both feel vague. They apply whichever formula feels closer.
Fix: Use the one-question triage above. Before you write anything, ask: "Am I finding the value of a definite integral, or am I differentiating a function that is defined as an integral?" That single question routes you to the right part every time. Two separate skills. Two separate operations.

Consequence: On FRQ Part C, students who could not name which theorem they were applying lost the justification point even when their arithmetic was correct. Missing that point is the difference between a 4 and a 5 for students in the 60–67 composite range.
Mistake 2: Forgetting the chain rule when the upper bound is a function
Why it happens: The basic version, $\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$, is clean and memorable. So when students see a more complex upper bound, they apply the same clean formula and stop. They get $f(x^2)$ instead of $f(x^2) \cdot 2x$.
Fix: Every time the upper bound is anything other than plain $x$, write out the chain rule step explicitly. $u = g(x)$, then $f(u) \cdot g'(x)$. Do not skip the multiplication step.

Consequence: This is an MCQ point that should take under 90 seconds. Missing it because you dropped the chain rule means you spent the time and still got it wrong. The wrong answer choice usually corresponds exactly to the result you get if you forget the $g'(x)$ factor.
Mistake 3: Leaving the dummy variable in the answer
Why it happens: The FTC Part 1 problems all use $t$ as the integration variable. After applying the theorem, students copy $f(t)$ into their answer instead of converting to $f(x)$.
Fix: Immediately after applying Part 1, replace every $t$ with $x$ (or with $g(x)$ if chain rule applies). The $t$ is a placeholder. It has no meaning outside the integral. If $t$ appears in your final answer, that is a red flag.

Consequence: On MCQ, your result still has $t$ in it and does not match any answer choice. You second-guess yourself, waste 90 seconds, and often switch to the wrong answer.

Practice Problems

Try these before checking the answers. For each one, identify which part of the FTC applies before you write a single symbol.

Problem 1: Evaluation

$$\int_0^3 (x^2 - 4x + 7)\,dx$$

Problem 2: FTC Part 1 with Chain Rule

Let $G(x) = \displaystyle\int_1^{x^3} \sin(t^2)\,dt$. Find $G'(x)$.

Problem 3: Variable in Lower Bound

Let $F(x) = \displaystyle\int_x^{10} (t^2 + 1)\,dt$. Find $F'(x)$.

Answers
Problem 1: $F(x) = \frac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 + 7x$. $F(3) = 9 - 18 + 21 = 12$. $F(0) = 0$. Answer: $12$.
Problem 2: Upper bound is $x^3$. Chain rule: $G'(x) = \sin((x^3)^2) \cdot 3x^2 = 3x^2\sin(x^6)$.
Problem 3: Variable in lower bound. Flip and negate: $F(x) = -\int_{10}^x(t^2+1)\,dt$. So $F'(x) = -(x^2+1)$.

FAQ

Q: What is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus in AP Calculus, and how many parts are there?+

There are 2 parts. Part 1 says that if $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $F'(x) = f(x)$. Part 2 (the Evaluation Theorem) says $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$. Both are in scope for AB and BC. Part 2 shows up every time you compute a definite integral. Part 1 shows up in MCQ and FRQ as a standalone problem type.

Know both and know which is which. The AP exam will not tell you which one to use.

Q: When do I use FTC Part 1 vs. Part 2 on the AP exam?+

If the problem gives you a function defined as an integral and asks for its derivative, use Part 1. If it asks you to compute the value of a definite integral, use Part 2. The expression tells you which one: $\frac{d}{dx}[\text{integral}]$ is Part 1. $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx =$ a number is Part 2. The hardest MCQ variants give you both in the same problem. Stay calm and do one step at a time.

If you see $\frac{d}{dx}$ in front of an integral sign, you are in Part 1 territory. That single symbol is the triage.

Q: How does the chain rule apply to FTC Part 1?+

When the upper bound is a function of $x$ (like $x^2$, $\sin x$, or $3x+1$), you apply the chain rule on top of Part 1: $\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{g(x)} f(t)\,dt = f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$. Memorize that template. The AP exam tests this version specifically, and the wrong answer choices are designed to catch students who apply Part 1 without the chain rule factor.

This is one of the most learnable 1-point MCQ gains on the entire exam. Recognize the pattern, apply it in under 90 seconds, automatic point.

Q: Do I need to know the proof of the FTC for AP Calculus?+

No. The AP exam does not ask you to prove either part. It asks you to apply them, identify them by name in justifications, and recognize which one a problem is calling for. What you need cold: the statement of both parts, the chain rule extension of Part 1, and the FRQ justification language ("by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2").

Proofs are for your teacher to know. AP points come from application and justification. Focus there.
