The Mistake That Connects Three Functions

I have had students who could differentiate any function I put in front of them and still miss every single particle motion question on the free response, because they treat position, velocity, and acceleration as three separate ideas instead of one connected chain.

There is a specific kind of freeze I see on particle motion FRQs. Not the panicked freeze where a student does not recognize the topic at all. The more expensive freeze: a student who knows all three functions, knows how to differentiate, and still cannot answer "is the particle speeding up or slowing down at $t = 2$?" They stare at the numbers they just calculated and cannot connect them to the question. I call it the Disconnect. The math is there. The interpretation is not.

This is not a skill gap. It is a framing gap. Position, velocity, and acceleration are not three separate functions you happen to take derivatives of. They are one chain: $s(t)$ tells you where the particle is, $v(t) = s'(t)$ tells you how fast and in what direction, and $a(t) = v'(t)$ tells you how that direction is changing. The relationships run in both directions. Differentiate to go from position to velocity to acceleration. Integrate to go back.

Position, velocity, and acceleration in AP Calculus shows up on nearly every AP Calculus AB free response exam, often as Part A of a multi-part problem worth 8 or 9 points. It is one of the highest-yield skills you can build before May. This post teaches you the chain, the common traps, and exactly how the AP exam scores each part.

Screenshot-able one-liner
If you know where a particle is but not how fast it is changing direction, you do not know enough to answer the AP question.

What This Topic Actually Means

Your textbook introduces position, velocity, and acceleration as physics concepts that happen to use calculus. I have never found that framing useful for AP prep.

Here is the framing that actually helps. Imagine a point moving along a number line. Not a car on a road, not an object in space. Just a point on a line. The position function $s(t)$ tells you where that point is at any given moment $t$. It gives you a coordinate: $s(2) = 4$ means the point is 4 units to the right of the origin when $t = 2$.

The velocity function $v(t)$ tells you how fast that coordinate is changing at each instant. Positive velocity means the particle is moving right (position increasing). Negative velocity means the particle is moving left (position decreasing). Zero velocity means the particle is stopped at that exact moment.

The acceleration function $a(t)$ tells you how fast the velocity is changing. Positive acceleration means velocity is increasing. Negative acceleration means velocity is decreasing.

That chain of relationships is the whole topic.

$$v(t) = s'(t) \qquad a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)$$

AROC vs. IROC: The Distinction That Costs Points

This distinction comes up on every particle motion FRQ. Students lose points not because their math is wrong, but because they set up the wrong calculation.

Average Velocity = AROC

Average velocity over $[a, b]$: the average rate of change of position. A subtraction-and-division problem.

$$\text{Average velocity} = \frac{s(b) - s(a)}{b - a} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}$$
Instantaneous Velocity = IROC

Instantaneous velocity at $t$: the instantaneous rate of change of position. A derivative problem.

$$v(t) = s'(t) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{s(t + h) - s(t)}{h}$$

If a particle moves from $s(1) = 4$ to $s(3) = 0$, the average velocity is $\frac{0 - 4}{3 - 1} = -2$ units per second. That is AROC. But $v(2) = s'(2)$ for the specific function is a different number entirely. That is IROC.

The AP exam will ask for both. "Find the average velocity over $[0, 4]$" is a subtraction problem. "Find the velocity at $t = 2$" is a derivative problem. Students who treat these as the same question lose the setup point every time. This connects directly back to the core distinction in What Is a Derivative in AP Calculus: average rate of change looks at an interval, instantaneous rate of change collapses to a single point. Particle motion is where that distinction costs real exam points.

Speed vs. Velocity: Not the Same Thing

This is a dedicated FRQ question type. It shows up almost every year. Most students mix these up, and that costs them points.

The Definitions

Velocity $= v(t)$. Can be positive, negative, or zero. The sign encodes direction.

Speed $= |v(t)|$. Always non-negative. Direction information is gone.

The AP exam does not just test whether you know the definition. It asks you to determine whether the particle is speeding up or slowing down. Here is the rule that earns full credit every time:

Speeding Up vs. Slowing Down

Speeding up: $v(t)$ and $a(t)$ have the same sign. Both positive or both negative.

Slowing down: $v(t)$ and $a(t)$ have opposite signs. One positive, one negative.

Using $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$, here is the full sign chart for $v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9$ and $a(t) = 6t - 12$:

IntervalSign of $v(t)$Sign of $a(t)$Status
$(0, 1)$$+$$-$Slowing down
$(1, 2)$$-$$-$Speeding up
$(2, 3)$$-$$+$Slowing down
$(3, 5)$$+$$+$Speeding up

On the FRQ, your justification must include both signs: "At $t = 1.5$, $v(1.5) < 0$ and $a(1.5) < 0$, so the particle is speeding up because velocity and acceleration have the same sign." That is a full-credit justification. "The speed is increasing" without the sign analysis earns zero on the justification line.

The Method: Reading the Particle Motion Chain

Every particle motion FRQ follows the same set of question types. Know these five and you will recognize every variant the AP throws at you.

STEP 1 Find $v(t)$ by differentiating $s(t)$. Apply power rule, product rule, or chain rule as needed.
STEP 2 Find $a(t)$ by differentiating $v(t)$. Write it completely.
STEP 3 Find when the particle is at rest. Set $v(t) = 0$ and solve. These are the critical moments where direction potentially changes.
STEP 4 Determine direction and speed status. Build a sign chart for $v(t)$ and $a(t)$. Same signs = speeding up. Opposite signs = slowing down.
STEP 5 Find total distance traveled. Find zeros of $v(t)$ in the interval. Evaluate $s$ at each turning point. Add the absolute values of each leg. Do NOT use $s(b) - s(a)$ for total distance.

For $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$ on $[0, 4]$: particle starts at $s(0) = 0$, moves right to $s(1) = 4$ (4 units), moves left to $s(3) = 0$ (4 units), moves right to $s(4) = 4$ (4 units). Total distance $= 4 + 4 + 4 = 12$ units. Displacement $= s(4) - s(0) = 4$ units. Different numbers. The AP asks for both.

Worked Example 1: Foundational

The setup: a particle moves along a horizontal line with position given by $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$ for $t \geq 0$, where $t$ is measured in seconds and $s$ is measured in meters.

Find $v(t)$, find $a(t)$, determine when the particle is at rest, and state the direction of motion on $(0, 1)$.

Step 1 — Find v(t)

Differentiate $s(t)$ using the power rule:

$$v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9$$

Step 2 — Find a(t)

$$a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12$$

Step 3 — When is the particle at rest?

Set $v(t) = 0$:

$$3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 0 \implies 3(t-1)(t-3) = 0 \implies t = 1 \text{ and } t = 3$$

The particle is at rest at $t = 1$ second and at $t = 3$ seconds.

Step 4 — Direction of motion on (0, 1)

Test $t = 0.5$: $v(0.5) = 3(0.25) - 12(0.5) + 9 = 0.75 - 6 + 9 = 3.75 > 0$.

Velocity is positive on $(0, 1)$: the particle is moving in the positive direction (right) on that interval.

Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Find $v(t)$ by differentiating $s(t)$ correctly — 1 point
  • Find $a(t)$ by differentiating $v(t)$ correctly — 1 point
  • Set $v(t) = 0$, factor correctly, identify both $t = 1$ and $t = 3$ — 1 point
  • Test sign of $v(t)$ on $(0, 1)$ and state direction with justification — 1 point

Common error: stating "the particle is at rest at $t = 1$" without showing $v(1) = 0$ explicitly. Write the substitution. The grader awards points for shown work, not conclusions.

Worked Example 2: Full AP Particle Motion FRQ

This is the format you will see on the AP exam. Multi-part, scored rubric, explicit justification required on every part. Work through each part completely before checking the scoring.

Problem

A particle moves along the x-axis. The position of the particle at time $t$ is given by $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$ for $0 \leq t \leq 5$.

(a) Find $v(t)$. Find all values of $t$ in $[0, 5]$ for which the particle is at rest.

(b) Find $a(t)$. Is the particle speeding up or slowing down at $t = 0.5$? Give a reason for your answer.

(c) Find all intervals in $[0, 5]$ on which the particle is speeding up. Justify your answer.

(d) Find the total distance traveled by the particle from $t = 0$ to $t = 4$.

Solution — Part (a)

$$v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t-1)(t-3)$$

Set $v(t) = 0$: the particle is at rest at $\mathbf{t = 1}$ and $\mathbf{t = 3}$.

Solution — Part (b)

$$a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12$$

At $t = 0.5$:

$$v(0.5) = 3(0.25) - 6 + 9 = 3.75 > 0$$
$$a(0.5) = 6(0.5) - 12 = -9 < 0$$

Since $v(0.5) > 0$ and $a(0.5) < 0$, the signs are opposite. The particle is slowing down at $t = 0.5$.

Solution — Part (c)

The particle speeds up when $v(t)$ and $a(t)$ have the same sign.

Sign of $v(t) = 3(t-1)(t-3)$:

  • $v(t) > 0$ on $(0, 1) \cup (3, 5)$
  • $v(t) < 0$ on $(1, 3)$

Sign of $a(t) = 6(t - 2)$:

  • $a(t) < 0$ on $(0, 2)$
  • $a(t) > 0$ on $(2, 5)$
Interval$v(t)$$a(t)$Status
$(0, 1)$$+$$-$Slowing down
$(1, 2)$$-$$-$Speeding up
$(2, 3)$$-$$+$Slowing down
$(3, 5)$$+$$+$Speeding up

The particle is speeding up on $(1, 2)$ and $(3, 5)$.

Required justification (write this verbatim)

"On $(1, 2)$, $v(t) < 0$ and $a(t) < 0$, so the particle is speeding up because velocity and acceleration have the same sign. On $(3, 5)$, $v(t) > 0$ and $a(t) > 0$, so the particle is speeding up because velocity and acceleration have the same sign."

Solution — Part (d)

Turning points of $v(t)$ in $[0, 4]$: $t = 1$ and $t = 3$. Evaluate $s$ at each:

$$s(0) = 0 \qquad s(1) = 1 - 6 + 9 = 4 \qquad s(3) = 27 - 54 + 27 = 0 \qquad s(4) = 64 - 96 + 36 = 4$$

Leg 1: $s(0) = 0$ to $s(1) = 4$ → distance $= |4 - 0| = 4$ meters
Leg 2: $s(1) = 4$ to $s(3) = 0$ → distance $= |0 - 4| = 4$ meters
Leg 3: $s(3) = 0$ to $s(4) = 4$ → distance $= |4 - 0| = 4$ meters

$$\text{Total distance} = 4 + 4 + 4 = \boxed{12 \text{ meters}}$$

Displacement (not the same): $s(4) - s(0) = 4 - 0 = 4$ meters.

Here is exactly how the AP exam scores this type of problem
  • Part (a) — 2 pts: 1 for correct $v(t)$; 1 for both $t = 1$ and $t = 3$ with work shown
  • Part (b) — 2 pts: 1 for correct $a(t)$; 1 for correct sign analysis with both $v$ and $a$ signs stated explicitly
  • Part (c) — 3 pts: 1 for identifying critical values of $v$ and $a$; 1 for correct intervals; 1 for complete justification naming both signs in each interval
  • Part (d) — 3 pts: 1 for identifying turning points $t = 1$ and $t = 3$; 1 for correctly evaluating $s$ at each; 1 for summing absolute values of each leg

The most common way to lose points on Part (d): computing $s(4) - s(0) = 4$ and writing that as the answer. That is displacement. The AP makes this distinction on purpose. Using net change instead of absolute leg-by-leg sums leaves 2 points on the table.

The 3 Mistakes That Cost My Students Points

Mistake 1: Writing "speeding up" without a sign justification

Why it happens: students identify that speed is increasing intuitively and write the conclusion. The AP rubric awards a separate point for the justification. "Speeding up" without sign analysis earns zero on that line, even when the answer is correct.

The fix: every time you state speeding up or slowing down, write both signs. "$v(t) = -3 < 0$ and $a(t) = -6 < 0$; same sign; therefore speeding up." That is the full-credit structure. It takes five seconds and earns one point.

Mistake 2: Confusing total distance with displacement

Why it happens: $s(b) - s(a)$ is the first calculation that comes to mind. It feels right. It is wrong for total distance whenever the particle changes direction.

The fix: check for direction changes first. Find $v(t) = 0$ in the interval. If there are zeros, the particle reversed. Add up each leg separately using absolute values. Assume there are direction changes until you have verified otherwise.

Mistake 3: Treating every zero of velocity as a direction change

Why it happens: $v(t) = 0$ looks like a direction change. Sometimes it is. Sometimes it is just a momentary pause. Students mark every velocity zero as a reversal and get the distance calculation wrong.

The fix: when $v(t) = 0$ at time $t_0$, check the sign of $v(t)$ on both sides of $t_0$. If the sign changes, there is a direction reversal. If the sign stays the same on both sides, the particle paused and kept going. A sign chart is the cleanest tool here.

Practice Problems

Try these on your own before checking the answers. Set up the sign chart for $v(t)$ before you answer any question about direction or speed.

Problem 1 — Direction at a specific time

A particle has position $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$. Is the particle moving left or right at $t = 2$? At $t = 4$?

Answer: $v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9$. $v(2) = 12 - 24 + 9 = -3 < 0$: moving left at $t = 2$. $v(4) = 48 - 48 + 9 = 9 > 0$: moving right at $t = 4$.

Problem 2 — Speeding up or slowing down

Using the same function, is the particle speeding up or slowing down at $t = 2.5$? Write the complete justification.

Answer: $v(2.5) = 3(6.25) - 30 + 9 = -2.25 < 0$. $a(2.5) = 6(2.5) - 12 = 3 > 0$. Opposite signs. The particle is slowing down at $t = 2.5$ because $v(2.5) < 0$ and $a(2.5) > 0$.

Problem 3 — Total distance

A particle has position $s(t) = t^2 - 4t + 3$ on $[0, 5]$. Find the total distance traveled.

Answer: $v(t) = 2t - 4 = 0$ at $t = 2$. $s(0) = 3$. $s(2) = 4 - 8 + 3 = -1$. $s(5) = 25 - 20 + 3 = 8$. Distance $[0, 2]$: $|-1 - 3| = 4$ units. Distance $[2, 5]$: $|8 - (-1)| = 9$ units. Total distance $= 4 + 9 = \mathbf{13}$ units.

FAQ

Q: What is the difference between velocity and speed on the AP exam?+

Velocity is $v(t)$ — it carries a sign that encodes direction. Speed is $|v(t)|$ — always non-negative, direction information gone. When the AP asks "find the speed at $t = 2$," take the absolute value of $v(2)$. When it asks for velocity, keep the sign. These are not interchangeable, and the AP has asked both in the same problem.

Q: What does it mean when $v(t) = 0$?+

The particle is instantaneously at rest. It is not necessarily changing direction. Check whether $v(t)$ changes sign across that point. If it does, the particle reverses. If it does not, the particle paused and kept going the same direction. Always check both sides with a sign chart or test value. This is a direct FRQ scoring point — do not skip it.

Q: Why do I add absolute values for total distance instead of using $s(b) - s(a)$?+

$s(b) - s(a)$ gives displacement: the net change in position. If the particle moved 5 right, then 5 left, and ended where it started, displacement is 0 but total distance is 10. The AP distinguishes these every year. Use displacement for net change, absolute values per leg for total travel. Getting these mixed up costs 2 points in a common exam configuration.

Q: How does particle motion connect to integrals?+

Position, velocity, and acceleration form a chain that works in both directions. Differentiate to go down the chain. Integrate to go back up. $\int_a^b v(t)\,dt$ gives displacement. $\int_a^b |v(t)|\,dt$ gives total distance. You will see this connection again in Unit 6 and it is one of the most tested cross-unit relationships on the AP exam.

The chain

$$s(t) \xrightarrow{\;d/dt\;} v(t) \xrightarrow{\;d/dt\;} a(t)$$

Velocity sign rules

  • $v(t) > 0$: moving in positive direction
  • $v(t) < 0$: moving in negative direction
  • $v(t) = 0$: at rest (check for direction change)

Speed and acceleration

  • $v(t)$ and $a(t)$ same sign: speeding up
  • $v(t)$ and $a(t)$ opposite signs: slowing down
  • Speed $= |v(t)|$

AROC vs. IROC

$$\text{Average velocity} = \frac{s(b) - s(a)}{b - a} \qquad \text{Instantaneous velocity} = v(t) = s'(t)$$

Distance vs. displacement

  • Displacement: $s(b) - s(a)$
  • Total distance: sum of $|s(t_i) - s(t_{i-1})|$ for each leg (split at zeros of $v$)

Justification template

"At $t = c$, $v(c) = $ ___ [sign] and $a(c) = $ ___ [sign]. Since they have [same / opposite] sign(s), the particle is [speeding up / slowing down]."
